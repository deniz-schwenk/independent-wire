Iran Reviews US Response to Peace Proposal as Gulf Allies' Airspace Denial Forces Washington Toward Diplomacy

Oil prices fell sharply on deal optimism while Tehran demands $270 billion in war reparations and Russia vetoes a UN resolution demanding Iran cease attacks on commercial shipping.

This article draws on 30 sources across ten languages and thirteen countries, giving it broad geographic reach, though no Iranian state media and no direct Iranian civilian voices appear despite UN-documented mass displacement inside Iran. The most significant unresolved gap is the absence of anti-war civil society perspectives, merchant seafarer voices, and Pentagon officials — groups directly affected by or directing the conflict — which leaves the narrative dominated by senior political figures and analysts. The article's own voice occasionally assigns causation or strategic motive ('directly causing,' 'centers on leverage') in places where the underlying facts are contested or could be attributed to a named source.

Iran is actively reviewing the US response to Tehran's own 14-point peace proposal, delivered through Pakistani intermediaries, according to Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei, who described the American terms as difficult to assess due to what Tehran considers unrealistic demands [17]. The diplomatic opening followed reports that Saudi Arabia and Kuwait suspended US access to critical airbases and airspace, directly causing President Donald Trump to halt the naval escort plan known as "Project Freedom" in the Strait of Hormuz [13][16]. A source close to the Saudi government denied the account, stating simply, "This isn't true" [10], though the operational pause itself is confirmed across multiple outlets [6][13].

Global oil markets responded immediately. Brent crude dropped to $96.06 per barrel and West Texas Intermediate fell to $89.87 — a decline exceeding five percent — as traders priced in the possibility of a negotiated settlement [12]. Trump stated that a deal with Iran was "very possible" but warned of resumed military strikes if Tehran does not comply with US demands, which include a full halt to uranium enrichment [16][2]. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu continues to demand a total cessation of Iranian nuclear activities [2].

Tehran's negotiating posture centers on leverage. Iranian government spokesperson Fatemeh Mohajerani confirmed that war reparations of $270 billion are a core condition in the Islamabad talks [19]. Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi stated that Iran's international standing has improved since the conflict began: "Our Chinese friends believe that Iran after the war is different from Iran before the war" [2]. Analyst Saeed Leylaz argued that Iranian leadership has rallied around Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei and will not relinquish control of the Strait without a comprehensive agreement: "All of this signals to the Islamic Republic that if it gives up control of the strait without a strong geopolitical agreement, it would not be able to return and therefore it will lose" [2].

US officials frame the Iranian government differently. Secretary of State Marco Rubio described Iran's top officials as "insane in the brain" and characterized the leadership as fractured [2]. Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian moved to counter that narrative by publicly reporting his first meeting with Mojtaba Khamenei since the Supreme Leader was wounded in earlier strikes, describing him as humble and present [1][2]. Sina Toossi, a senior non-resident fellow at the Center for International Policy, assessed that "the military and security camp around Mojtaba Khamenei currently has enormous influence, arguably more than at any point in years because the war elevated the importance of coercive power, deterrence, and wartime cohesion" [2].

At the United Nations, the diplomatic fault line runs between the US-Gulf coalition and the Russia-China bloc. Washington and its Gulf allies proposed a Chapter VII resolution to compel Iran to clear mines and cease attacks on commercial shipping. Bahrain's UN ambassador Jamal Alrowaiei stated that "the draft resolution calls for Iran to immediately cease all attacks and threats against merchant and commercial vessels" [8]. Rubio framed the vote as existential for the institution: "If the international community can't rally behind this and solve something so straightforward, then I don't know what the utility of the UN system is" [4]. Russia's Permanent Representative Vasily Nebenzya vetoed the resolution, arguing it creates a dangerous precedent by targeting Iran without acknowledging that the maritime disruption was caused by US and Israeli military operations [22]. China's Foreign Ministry spokesperson Mao Ning called for a ceasefire and attributed blame for the crisis to the aggressors [24].

The economic damage extends far beyond oil markets. Moody's Analytics predicts Dubai hotel occupancy will plummet to ten percent in the second quarter of 2026, down from eighty percent before the war — "an effective shutdown of large parts of the hospitality sector" [9]. In Jordan, souvenir seller Khalid al-Saidat reported that the Iran conflict reduced foreign visitors to Petra to "almost zero" [3]. Shipping giant Maersk reported $500 million in added monthly costs from fuel price spikes and insurance premiums, though CEO Vincent Clerc noted the company passes those costs to customers [5]. Shell, meanwhile, posted first-quarter earnings of $6.92 billion, exceeding forecasts, with CEO Wael Sawan attributing results to "operational performance in a quarter marked by unprecedented disruption in global energy markets" [7]. Russian analysts estimate Moscow could receive $40 billion in windfall oil revenues from the conflict [23].

The crisis disproportionately threatens energy-importing nations in Asia. Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi warned of acute energy shortages across the Asia-Pacific [26]. Chinese media analyzed national resilience strategies including land-based pipelines and strategic petroleum reserves that partially mitigate the Hormuz blockade [25]. India is exploring diversification options including the Cape of Good Hope shipping route [30]. Iran's parliament has legislated a transit fee of one dollar per barrel for tankers passing through the strait [27].

The UAE has established a national committee to document Iranian strikes — including over 2,800 drones and missiles targeting landmarks and energy facilities — to support future legal claims for reparations [11]. The UN Human Rights Council has documented the destruction of schools, hospitals, and World Heritage sites inside Iran, along with mass displacement of civilians [28].

Within the United States, Senate Democrats have repeatedly attempted to invoke the War Powers Resolution, accusing the administration of waging an unauthorized sixty-day war. The administration argues that ceasefire pauses reset the legal clock, creating an unresolved constitutional standoff [18]. Trump questioned the purpose of the UN itself, suggesting his proposed "Board of Peace" could eventually replace it [4].

The US response to Iran's 14-point peace proposal is under active review in Tehran, with further developments expected through the Pakistani channel, as oil markets and Gulf economies remain poised to react to any signal of progress or breakdown [17][6].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
