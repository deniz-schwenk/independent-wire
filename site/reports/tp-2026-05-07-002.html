Hantavirus Outbreak on MV Hondius Cruise Ship Kills Three as WHO Coordinates Contact Tracing Across 12 Countries

The WHO assesses overall public health risk as low, but the identification of the Andes strain—capable of human-to-human transmission—has triggered an urgent multinational effort to locate disembarked passengers.

The World Health Organization confirmed at least five cases and as many as eight—with three deaths—from hantavirus infection linked to the expedition cruise ship MV Hondius, with case counts varying across reporting dates: the WHO's own disease outbreak notice reported seven cases [3], while later reporting cited eight [11], and earlier reporting five [1]. The pathogen was identified as the Andes strain, triggering contact-tracing operations spanning at least 12 countries [1][3]. The ship, operated by Oceanwide Expeditions, had been sailing in the South Atlantic when the cluster emerged among passengers, some of whom disembarked at Saint Helena before the outbreak's scope became clear [8][7].

WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus stated: " While this is a serious incident, WHO assesses the public health risk as low" [1]. WHO officials were careful to distinguish the outbreak from the COVID-19 pandemic. Maria Van Kerkhove, WHO's director for epidemic and pandemic preparedness and prevention, said: "This is not SARS-CoV-2. This is not the start of a COVID pandemic" [2]. Abdirahman Mahamud, WHO's director for health emergency alert and response operations, added: "We don't anticipate a large epidemic with the experience our member states have and the actions they have taken" [2].

Unlike most hantavirus strains, the Andes variant is capable of human-to-human transmission among close contacts, a characteristic that elevated the urgency of the international response even as officials sought to contain public alarm [4]. Argentine authorities are reconstructing the pre-boarding travel itinerary of the initial victims—reported to be a Dutch couple who traveled through Chile, Argentina, and Uruguay on a birdwatching trip—to identify the precise point of infection from rodent reservoirs [5][1].

The contact-tracing effort has taken on particular urgency because of a specific flight: WHO is urgently seeking the 83 passengers of Airlink flight 4Z132 who may have been exposed to a symptomatic traveler en route to Johannesburg from Saint Helena [7]. A second case died in South Africa after disembarking at Saint Helena [3]. Spain's Health Minister Mónica García announced the tracking of 23 passengers who disembarked early and confirmed that 14 Spanish passengers currently aboard remain asymptomatic [6]. Three suspected cases were medically evacuated to the Netherlands [11], and the ship was directed toward the Canary Islands for further medical screening [10].

Oceanwide Expeditions stated it has been in contact with all passengers since March 20 to monitor their health [8]. The US CDC is tracking American passengers from the vessel across at least three states, including a resident of Arizona, and assesses community transmission risk as low [12]. A Japanese national was also confirmed to be among those on board [11]. Italy's Ministry of Health clarified that no Italian passengers were on the vessel [9].
