Iran has threatened to disrupt shipping through the Red Sea and Bab al-Mandab strait in addition to its existing restrictions on the Strait of Hormuz, a move that European aviation authorities say could trigger systemic jet fuel shortages at EU airports within three weeks [1][4]. The threat represents a material escalation in the US-Iran conflict, which began in late February 2026 and has already produced a US naval blockade of Iranian ports, a failed round of high-level peace talks in Islamabad, and a fragile ceasefire set to expire on April 22 [2][12].

Forty percent of Europe's jet fuel imports transit the Strait of Hormuz, and no cargoes have arrived since the war began, according to ACI Europe, the continent's airport trade association [1]. Jet fuel prices across Europe have doubled, and airports have already begun implementing fuel rationing [5][10]. Airlines for Europe (A4E), the airline industry lobby, is demanding urgent emergency measures from the EU [5]. French-language reporting indicates that Ryanair and Air France-KLM are planning flight cancellations as the supply situation deteriorates [10].

The distinction between crude oil and refined fuel is central to understanding the crisis. German-language Euronews reported that the EU holds 90 days of crude oil reserves, and the International Energy Agency has released 400 million barrels from strategic stocks to contain price spikes [3]. However, these measures address crude oil, not the refined kerosene that aircraft require. As Euronews reported: 'Was die Sperrung der Straße von Hormus für Ölpreise, Gas und...' (What the closure of the Strait of Hormuz means for oil prices, gas, and...) — the vulnerability lies specifically in refined product supply chains [3]. Some states have as little as eight to ten days of jet fuel supply remaining [1].

Iran's framing of the maritime disruptions differs sharply from the European crisis narrative. Ali Akbar Velayati, senior advisor to Iran's Supreme Leader, stated that the Strait of Hormuz remains open to the world but is permanently closed to 'enemies' of Iran and their military interests [13]. Iranian and Persian-language sources describe the Bab al-Mandab threat as a defensive response to the possibility that the United States might attempt to occupy Kharg Island, Iran's primary oil export terminal [4]. Radio Zamaneh reported: 'باب‌المندب، سلاح جدید ایران در برابر احتمال اشغال خارگ' (Bab al-Mandab, Iran's new weapon against the possibility of Kharg occupation) [4]. Euronews Arabic detailed Iran's warning that it would halt all exports and imports through the Persian Gulf, Gulf of Oman, and the Red Sea if the US naval blockade continues [9].

European and Western sources treat the Red Sea threat primarily as an energy supply emergency. Iranian and Arabic-language sources treat it as a strategic military lever [4][9][1][10]. The EU's naval mission Aspides has warned of increased Houthi attacks in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden, assessing high risk for vessels linked to the United States or Israel and medium risk for other shipping [15]. As Verkehrs Rundschau reported: 'EU-Mission warnt vor steigender Gefahr für Schifffahrt im Roten Meer' (EU mission warns of increasing danger for shipping in the Red Sea) [15].

Not all energy-dependent states face the same vulnerability. Taiwan has bypassed the Hormuz chokepoint entirely by sourcing oil from Saudi Red Sea ports and maintains 140 days of reserves, according to Connaissance des Énergies [11]. This adaptation strategy has received virtually no coverage outside French-language media.

The diplomatic picture remains fractured. High-level talks in Islamabad on April 11-12 between US Vice President JD Vance and Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf ended without agreement after 21 hours of negotiations [14][2]. Time Magazine reported that the US offered sanctions relief in exchange for nuclear guarantees, which Iran rejected [2]. Iranian analyst Mohammad Marandi, quoted by Tabnak, argued that the Trump administration approached the talks with 'maximalist' demands and did not negotiate seriously [7]. Robert Malley, referenced in the same report, confirmed that no agreement was reached [7].

Turkish and Chinese sources emphasize the ongoing potential for diplomacy. Anadolu Agency highlighted the constructive role of regional powers including Turkey, Egypt, and Saudi Arabia in facilitating the Islamabad process [6]. People's Daily reported that US President Donald Trump suggested talks might resume within two days, expressing optimism that the war is 'close to over' [8]. As People's Daily reported: '特朗普暗示美伊"未来两天"或重启伊斯兰堡会谈' (Trump hints US and Iran may restart Islamabad talks 'within two days') [8]. Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif is actively working to involve Turkey, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia in a broader multilateral mediation effort [12]. Iranian sources, by contrast, frame the same diplomatic moment as one where Iran successfully resisted US pressure [7][13].
