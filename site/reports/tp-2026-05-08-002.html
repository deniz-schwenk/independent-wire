Russia's Victory Day Parade Excludes Military Hardware for First Time in Nearly Two Decades as Ukrainian Drones Strike Deep Inside Russian Territory

Moscow and Kyiv trade accusations of ceasefire violations while 13 Russian airports suspend operations after a drone hits an air traffic control center in Rostov-on-Don.

This article draws on 44 sources across eight languages, but roughly 57 percent originate from Ukrainian outlets, and the overall narrative gives more structural weight to the Ukrainian strategic framing of events than to Russian or neutral accounts. No U.S. officials are quoted despite Washington's central role in brokering the ceasefire, no independent humanitarian organizations verify either side's ceasefire-violation figures, and ordinary Russian and Ukrainian civilians affected by airport closures, internet shutdowns, and ongoing strikes are entirely absent. The article's own voice occasionally amplifies judgments — describing the Kremlin's "acute fear," the "diminished" parade, and the war's "spillover" — that would read more neutrally if attributed to specific analysts or replaced with factual description.

Russia's May 9 Victory Day parade on Red Square featured no tanks, missiles, or military vehicles for the first time in nearly twenty years, as authorities cited security concerns amid an intensifying Ukrainian drone campaign that struck oil refineries, an air traffic control center, and infrastructure across multiple Russian regions [3][7]. The scaled-back ceremony — attended by only three foreign leaders — unfolded against a backdrop of mutual accusations between Moscow and Kyiv over who violated a short ceasefire declared around the holiday [2][4].

The Russian Defense Ministry stated that its forces "completely ceased combat operations" when the ceasefire took effect and accused Ukraine of 1,365 violations, including 887 UAV strikes and 153 artillery attacks [4]; a later Sputnik India report cited the Russian ministry as claiming 1,173 violations [42]. Kremlin spokesperson Dmitry Peskov attributed the absence of military hardware at the parade to a "terrorist threat" rather than any sign of weakness [40]. Sputnik India reported the Russian ministry's claim of strict ceasefire adherence while characterizing Ukrainian strikes as unprovoked aggression [42].

President Volodymyr Zelenskyy presented a sharply different account. He stated that Russia carried out "more than 140 strikes on frontline positions" and over 850 drone attacks overnight, asserting that "on the Russian side, there was not even a token attempt to cease fire on the front" [4][24]. Ukraine framed its retaliatory strikes on Russian energy infrastructure as justified defensive responses, with Zelenskyy confirming a hit on the Slavneft-YANOS oil refinery in Yaroslavl — more than 700 kilometers from the Ukrainian border — calling it "an oil sector facility that was of great importance for financing Russia's war" [20][44]. Independent verification of either side's ceasefire violation figures remains unavailable, and no international monitoring body has adjudicated the competing claims [4].

The concrete impact of Ukrainian drone operations extended well beyond the front lines. A drone struck the air traffic control center in Rostov-on-Don, damaging equipment and forcing 13 airports across southern Russia to suspend operations, stranding at least 14,000 passengers [8][13][37]. Russia's Transportation Ministry announced that personnel were safe and that an assessment of equipment was underway, while Transportation Minister Andrey Nikitin asked airlines to coordinate alternative ground transport [8][13]. Moscow's Vnukovo Airport also suspended flights, with more than 50 delays or cancellations reported [13]. Mobile internet was shut down across at least 21 Russian regions, a measure Russian telecom operators Beeline and MTS attributed to the need to hinder drone navigation systems [7][39].

Analysts described the parade's transformation as driven by the Kremlin's acute fear of the symbolic consequences of a drone strike on Russia's most important national holiday. Oleg Ignatov, senior Russia analyst at Crisis Group, said that "for modern Russia, it's the main holiday of the year" and that authorities feared the political and psychological fallout of even a small drone reaching the event [3]. Olha Polishchuk of the Armed Conflict Location & Event Data project noted that "drones are indeed the primary means to attack Russia's territory" and that the Victory Day parade had been "twisted in modern Russia to support aggressive behaviour and militarisation" [3]. At least 11 Russian regions cancelled public events entirely, and the traditional Immortal Regiment marches were scrapped nationwide [7]. The Russian news outlet Fontanka reported specific cancellations across multiple cities, noting the absence of military hardware in Moscow for the first time since 2007 [36].

Ukrainian and Western-aligned analysts framed the scaled-back celebrations as evidence of broader Russian strategic difficulty. The Institute for the Study of War assessed that Russian forces suffered a net loss of 116 square kilometers of territory in April, with the rate of advance slowing by at least two-thirds over 18 months [1]. Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskii reported that Russia was "urgently, outside approved plans, deploying four more regiments, 24 battalions, and 162 batteries" to counter Ukrainian strike drones [14]. Dutch strategic analyst Patrick Bolder of the Hague Centre for Strategic Studies stated: "Now, I think, we've come to a point where the tides are really turning against Russia and in favor of Ukraine" [11]. Zelenskyy estimated at least $7 billion in damage to Russia's oil industry since the start of the year [1].

The holiday also exposed a deeper contest over historical memory. Ukraine now marks May 8 as the Day of Remembrance and Victory over Nazism, having legislatively shifted away from the Soviet-era May 9 observance [25]. Zelenskyy described Russia's aggression as "an updated version of Nazism, marked: made in Russia" [18]. A Kyiv district court sentenced Russian textbook editors Vladimir Medinsky and Anatoly Torkunov to ten years in prison in absentia for justifying armed aggression through a state-approved history textbook [15].

The war's spillover reached NATO territory when two drones, assessed as likely Ukrainian, crashed in Latvia — one igniting a fire at an oil storage facility. Latvia's foreign ministry summoned a Russian diplomat and rejected Moscow's accusation that Latvia had permitted its airspace to be used for attacks on St. Petersburg, stating it "has never granted permission to use its airspace to launch drone attacks against targets in Russia" [6]. Latvia's military warned that "as long as Russian aggression against Ukraine continues, it is possible that such incidents will be repeated" [9].

Foreign attendance at the diminished parade drew scrutiny. Only Belarusian President Alexander Lukashenko, Kazakhstan's President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev, and the King of Malaysia attended, with Radio Azattyq analyzing Central Asian leaders' presence as a geopolitical balancing act driven by the need to avoid offending Moscow [43][34]. Slovak Prime Minister Robert Fico traveled to Moscow but stated he would "not go to any military parade," instead laying flowers and holding a short meeting with Putin — potentially carrying messages from Zelenskyy [5].

Ukraine's Air Force spokesperson Colonel Yurii Ihnat described a "starvation ration" for air defense missiles, with launchers half-empty and commanders forced to redistribute scarce stocks among regions [31]. Separately, Zelenskyy announced that following talks by NSDC Secretary Rustem Umerov in the United States, American presidential envoys are expected in Kyiv by late spring or early summer [10]. The Netherlands is pushing for renewed EU talks on using frozen Russian sovereign assets to cover Ukraine's budget shortfall, though Belgium and the European Central Bank have raised objections [12].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
