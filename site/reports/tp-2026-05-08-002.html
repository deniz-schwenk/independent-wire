Russia Holds Victory Day Parade Without Tanks or Missiles as Ukrainian Drones Strike Deep Into Russian Territory

Moscow excluded military hardware from Red Square for the first time in nearly two decades while both sides accused each other of violating a declared ceasefire and Ukrainian strikes paralyzed 13 airports across southern Russia.

This article draws on 47 sources across nine languages, but more than half originate from Ukrainian outlets, and no Russian civilians affected by airport closures, internet shutdowns, or drone strikes on their cities are quoted — leaving the human cost on Russia's population entirely absent. No international humanitarian organizations assess the legality or civilian-protection implications of either side's ceasefire violations or strikes on air traffic infrastructure. The article's own voice occasionally amplifies events with terms like "massive," "paralyzing," and "extraordinary" beyond what the cited data requires, subtly reinforcing the framing of Ukrainian strategic momentum over the more neutral accounts found in some non-Ukrainian sources.

Russia's May 9 Victory Day parade proceeded on Red Square without tanks, missiles, or other military hardware for the first time in nearly twenty years, as the Russian Defense Ministry cited the "current operational situation" for the exclusion [7][38]. Simultaneously, a massive Ukrainian drone campaign struck oil refineries hundreds of kilometers from the border and hit an air traffic control center in Rostov-on-Don, paralyzing aviation across southern Russia and affecting more than 14,000 passengers [8][13][34].

The scaled-back parade unfolded against a backdrop of competing ceasefire claims. Russia declared a unilateral truce for the holiday, with the Russian Defense Ministry stating that its forces ceased operations but were compelled to respond after Ukrainian armed forces launched more than 1,365 violations including drone strikes and artillery fire on border regions [4]. President Volodymyr Zelenskyy rejected this account, stating that "on the Russian side, there was not even a token attempt to cease fire on the front" and reporting over 850 Russian drone attacks recorded across the frontline [24][2]. Ukraine's representative to the OSCE characterized the Russian ceasefire proposal as a cynical attempt to protect the Moscow parade while continuing attacks on civilians [39], and Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha accused Russia of rejecting peace efforts [43].

The absence of military hardware from Red Square drew sharply divergent interpretations. Crisis Group's senior Russia analyst Oleg Ignatov described the decision as driven by fear that a Ukrainian drone strike on tanks or missile launchers during the parade would deliver a devastating psychological blow, while ACLED research manager Olha Polishchuk noted that "drones are indeed the primary means to attack Russia's territory" [3]. Le Monde framed the reduction as a direct result of Ukrainian drone capabilities [40], while Der Spiegel connected it to Russian equipment losses on the battlefield [38]. Reuters' Japanese-language service included analysis from a former defense attaché linking the parade changes to broader battlefield pressures [46]. The Kremlin offered a different explanation: press secretary Dmitry Peskov stated that the decision was a responsible precaution against what he characterized as a shift toward terrorist tactics by the Ukrainian government [33].

Ukraine's long-range strikes during the holiday period targeted infrastructure deep inside Russia. President Zelenskyy confirmed a drone attack on the Slavneft-YANOS oil refinery in Yaroslavl — more than 700 kilometers from the Ukrainian border — calling it a "manifestation of justice" and describing such operations as "long-range sanctions" against Russia's war-financing capacity [20][4][35]. The strike on the Rostov-on-Don air navigation center forced the suspension of operations at 13 regional airports, with Russia's Transportation Ministry coordinating emergency rail and bus services for stranded travelers [8][34]. Governor Dmitry Makhonin of Perm Krai confirmed that drones also struck industrial sites in his region [20]. OilPrice.com reported a separate strike on the 400,000-barrel-per-day Kirishi refinery near St. Petersburg [45].

The drone war's geographic reach raised concerns beyond the immediate conflict zone. Latvia summoned a Russian diplomat after Moscow accused Ukraine of using Latvian airspace to launch An-196 Liutyi long-range drones against St. Petersburg [6][9]. Latvia's foreign ministry rejected the claim, stating it "has never granted permission to use its airspace to launch drone attacks against targets in Russia" [6]. Latvia's military warned that "as long as Russian aggression against Ukraine continues, it is possible that such incidents will be repeated" [9]. Two drones had crashed on Latvian territory near Rezekne, which Latvian officials assessed were Ukrainian assets en route to Russian targets [9].

Several analysts argued the broader war may be reaching a turning point. The Institute for the Study of War reported that Russian forces suffered a net loss of 116 square kilometers in April [1]. Ukrainian Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskii stated that Russia is "urgently, outside approved plans, deploying four more regiments, 24 battalions, and 162 batteries to counter our strike drones" [14]. Strategic analyst Patrick Bolder of the Hague Centre for Strategic Studies said the tides are "really turning against Russia and in favor of Ukraine" [11]. Zelenskyy stated that Russia has lost at least $7 billion in oil revenue since the beginning of the year from strikes on refining infrastructure [1].

Russia implemented extraordinary domestic security measures for the holiday. Mobile internet was shut down in at least 21 regions to disrupt drone navigation, and 11 regions canceled public celebrations entirely [7][47]. The parade itself drew limited international attendance, with Slovak Prime Minister Robert Fico traveling to Moscow but explicitly stating he would not attend the military parade, instead meeting Putin separately, with Slovak foreign ministry State Secretary Rastislav Chovanec telling parliament that Fico may deliver messages from Zelenskyy to Putin [5].

The symbolic dimensions of the day were contested along national lines. Zelenskyy marked May 8 as Ukraine's Day of Remembrance and Victory over Nazism — a legislative shift away from the Soviet-era May 9 date — and described the current Russian regime as "an updated version of Nazism, marked: made in Russia" [18][25]. British historian Geoffrey Roberts noted that Victory Day "will be celebrated as a Soviet as well as a Russian victory — the result of the common struggle of all the peoples of the multinational USSR, not least millions of Ukrainians" [3].

Meanwhile, Ukrainian Air Force spokesperson Yurii Ihnat warned that the country is on a "starvation ration" regarding air defense missiles, with launchers operating at half capacity and officials forced to request batches as small as five missiles for Patriot, NASAMS, and IRIS-T systems [31]. Diplomatic channels remain active: Zelenskyy announced expectations for U.S. envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner to visit Kyiv in late spring or early summer [10], and the Netherlands is pushing within the EU to resume discussions on using frozen Russian assets to fund Ukraine's defense [12].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
