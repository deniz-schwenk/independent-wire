Russia Excludes Military Hardware From Victory Day Parade for First Time in Nearly Two Decades as Ukrainian Drones Strike Deep Into Russian Territory

Moscow shut down mobile internet across 21 regions and canceled public events in 11 others while both sides accused each other of violating a declared ceasefire during the May 9 holiday.

This article draws on 47 sources in nine languages, but more than half originate from Ukrainian outlets, and no Russian civilians, independent Russian civil society voices, or major Western wire services (Reuters, AP, BBC) are represented — leaving the domestic Russian experience of drone strikes, airport closures, and internet shutdowns largely invisible. The article's own voice occasionally adopts evaluative language ("devastating," "paralyzed," "intense") that tilts toward dramatizing Ukrainian operational success, reinforcing a framing that analysts in the piece already note leans toward the Ukrainian/Western analytical perspective. Competing ceasefire timelines — who proposed what and when — remain only partially clarified, and a factual discrepancy over the number of flights disrupted (80 vs. 180) is unresolved.

Russia's Victory Day parade on May 9 proceeded without tanks, missiles, or other military hardware on Red Square for the first time in nearly 20 years, as Ukrainian long-range drones struck oil refineries and an air traffic control center hundreds of kilometers from the border during the holiday period [3][7][8]. The Russian Defense Ministry attributed the exclusion to the "current operational situation" [38], while analysts in multiple countries interpreted the decision as driven by fear that a successful Ukrainian drone strike on displayed weaponry would deliver a devastating psychological blow to the Kremlin's legitimacy [40][44][46].

The scaled-back celebrations extended well beyond Moscow. Eleven Russian regions canceled public events entirely, and authorities implemented mobile internet shutdowns in at least 21 regions to disrupt drone navigation systems [7][47]. Kremlin spokesperson Dmitry Peskov stated the hardware exclusion was intended to minimize risks amid what he characterized as a shift toward terrorism by the Ukrainian government [33]. Der Spiegel analyzed the parade reduction as reflecting both Russian military losses in Ukraine and a shortage of available reserves [38], while Le Monde framed it as a direct consequence of Ukrainian drone capabilities that the official "operational situation" narrative sought to obscure [40].

The holiday period saw an intense exchange of drone strikes despite competing ceasefire declarations. President Volodymyr Zelenskyy reported that Russia violated its own ceasefire with over 140 strikes and 10 assault operations on the morning of May 8, stating: "All of this clearly shows that, on the Russian side, there was not even a token attempt to cease fire on the front" [24]. Ukraine's representative to the OSCE characterized the Russian ceasefire proposal as a cynical attempt to protect the Moscow parade while continuing attacks on civilians [39]. Euronews reported that Russia launched 108 drones and 3 missiles during the declared truce period [43].

The Russian Defense Ministry presented a sharply different account, stating that its forces ceased operations as declared but were compelled to respond after Ukraine launched over 1,365 violations including artillery and drone strikes on civilian targets in Belgorod and Kursk regions [4]. Moscow reported downing 264 Ukrainian drones across multiple regions [2][13]. The mutual accusations spanned multiple languages and regions, with Turkish, French, German, and Dutch-language outlets all reporting the competing claims [41][43][39].

Ukraine conducted a coordinated campaign against Russian energy infrastructure during the holiday. Zelenskyy confirmed strikes on the Slavneft-YANOS oil refinery in Yaroslavl — more than 700 kilometers from the Ukrainian border — describing it as a "manifestation of justice" and characterizing such operations as "long-range sanctions" that have cost Russia at least $7 billion in oil revenue since the start of the year [20][4][1]. Additional strikes targeted industrial sites in Rostov-on-Don and Perm, with Perm Governor Dmitry Makhonin confirming drone impacts on industrial facilities [20]. OilPrice.com reported a separate strike on the 400,000-barrel-per-day Kirishi refinery near St. Petersburg [45]. Russian Finance Minister Anton Siluanov, however, projected that oil revenues would increase in May despite the campaign, suggesting the economic impact may be overstated [1].

A Ukrainian drone strike on the air traffic control center in Rostov-on-Don paralyzed aviation across southern Russia, suspending operations at 13 regional airports and affecting over 14,000 passengers [8][34]. Russia's Transportation Minister requested that airlines coordinate with railways and bus services to manage stranded travelers [8]. The Association of Tour Operators of Russia reported more than 80 flights delayed or canceled [13].

The drone war also created spillover risks for neighboring NATO states. Two Ukrainian drones crashed on Latvian territory while apparently transiting toward Russian targets, prompting Latvia to summon Russian Chargé d'Affaires Dmitry Kasatkin after Moscow accused Riga of allowing its airspace to be used for attacks on St. Petersburg [6][9]. Latvia's foreign ministry rejected the accusation, stating: "Contrary to Russia's false public claims, [Latvia] has never granted permission to use its airspace to launch drone attacks against targets in Russia" [6]. Latvia's military warned that such incidents would likely recur as long as Russian aggression continues [9].

Western and Ukrainian analysts pointed to broader shifts in the war's trajectory. The Institute for the Study of War reported that Russian forces suffered a net territorial loss of 116 square kilometers in April, noting that "Russian forces have been using infiltration tactics in part to create the perception of continuous Russian advances" [1]. Ukrainian Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskii stated that Russia is "urgently, outside approved plans, deploying four more regiments, 24 battalions, and 162 batteries to counter our strike drones" [14]. Patrick Bolder of the Hague Centre for Strategic Studies argued: "Now, I think, we've come to a point where the tides are really turning against Russia and in favor of Ukraine" [11].

Oleg Ignatov of the International Crisis Group noted that Victory Day remains "the main holiday of the year" for modern Russia [3], while historian Geoffrey Roberts observed that the day commemorates "the common struggle of all the peoples of the multinational USSR, not least millions of Ukrainians" [3]. Zelenskyy marked Ukraine's own May 8 Day of Remembrance by describing Russian aggression as "an updated version of Nazism, marked: made in Russia" [18].

Diplomatic channels remained active despite the battlefield escalation. Slovak Prime Minister Robert Fico traveled to Moscow for a meeting with Putin, potentially carrying messages from Zelenskyy, though he stated he would not attend the military parade [5]. Zelenskyy separately announced expectations for U.S. envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner to visit Kyiv in late spring or early summer to "reinvigorate diplomacy" [10].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
