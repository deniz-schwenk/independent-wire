France and UK Convene 40-Nation Paris Summit on Multinational Mission to Reopen Strait of Hormuz

The proposed defensive naval operation would exclude the United States, Iran, and Israel as belligerent parties, but implementation hinges on a ceasefire that has not yet held.

This article draws on 16 sources in 5 languages across 8 countries, and it surfaces important disagreements in how different regions frame the crisis — Western governments describe a defensive, peaceful mission while Chinese-language sources describe a coercive pressure campaign, and Arabic-language sources foreground the US naval blockade as a cause that UK officials omit from their framing. No Iranian or US government officials were available to provide their positions, and no Gulf Arab state officials, shipping operators, or communities directly affected by the energy disruption are represented — meaning the two nations most central to the crisis and the people most concretely harmed by it are absent from the reporting.

France and the United Kingdom convened a 40-nation summit in Paris on April 17, 2026, to discuss the formation of a multinational defensive mission aimed at restoring freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz [1][3][5]. The summit represents the first concrete multilateral response to the closure of the waterway, which has disrupted global oil transit since the US declared a naval blockade of Iranian ports in early April [11][13].

UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer framed the reopening as a "global responsibility" and called on participating nations to contribute to a multinational defensive mission to restore navigation [1]. French President Emmanuel Macron, co-hosting the summit, argued that a purely military reopening of the Strait is unrealistic and emphasized the need for diplomatic collaboration [2]. As Euronews reported in French: 'Paris et Séoul veulent contribuer à la réouverture du détroit d'Ormuz' (Paris and Seoul want to contribute to the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz) [2]. South Korean President Lee Jae Myung agreed to collaborate with France to mitigate the economic uncertainties caused by the conflict [2].

The summit's operational framework, as reported by the Saudi-focused outlet House of Saud, includes a specific mine-clearance mandate and explicitly excludes the United States, Iran, and Israel as "belligerent" nations from the operational mission [7]. La Tribune described the reopening as 'un casse-tête logistique, diplomatique et militaire' (a logistical, diplomatic, and military puzzle), noting that navigation security would require strict military control [9]. Le Monde characterized the entire mission as "largely symbolic," reporting that implementation cannot begin until US-Israeli hostilities with Iran end and a stable ceasefire takes hold beyond April 21 [12].

The legal foundation for the summit's mandate was articulated by Arsenio Dominguez, Secretary-General of the International Maritime Organization, who stated that under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, no country has the legal right to restrict navigation in the Strait of Hormuz [8].

The economic urgency driving the summit is acute. ACI Europe, the airports trade body, warned that European airports face a systemic jet fuel shortage within three weeks if the Strait remains closed [4]. Le Figaro reported: 'les aéroports européens redoutent des pénuries de kérosène d'ici trois semaines' (European airports fear kerosene shortages within three weeks) [4]. The International Energy Agency's chief separately warned that Europe has approximately six weeks of jet fuel reserves remaining, with some countries — including the United Kingdom and the Netherlands — holding significantly less [14]. Italian airports have already begun rationing fuel supplies [14].

Chinese-language sources framed the summit differently from Western organizers. NetEase highlighted diplomatic friction within the Western alliance, reporting that France refused to allow the NATO Secretary General to attend and that the United States was absent from the proceedings [5]. As the headline stated: '法国拒绝北约秘书长参会，美国缺席' (France refuses NATO Secretary General's attendance, US absent) [5]. Singapore's Lianhe Zaobao described the broader strategy as a UK-led coercive "three-pronged pressure" campaign — diplomatic, economic, and military — aimed at forcing Iran to reopen the waterway [10]. This framing contrasts with the UK and French governments' emphasis on a "purely defensive" and "peaceful" mission [1][2].

Arabic-language sources provided additional context largely absent from Western coverage. Anadolu Agency's Arabic service and the UAE-based Al-Ittihad both referenced the US naval blockade of Iranian ports as a key factor precipitating the crisis and the summit [3][11]. The UK government's official announcement, by contrast, framed the initiative around "freedom of navigation" and "maritime security" without acknowledging the US blockade as a contributing element [1].

Turkish coverage diverged from all other regional framings by treating the crisis primarily as a strategic opportunity. Turkey's Energy Minister Alparslan Bayraktar emphasized the country's existing pipeline infrastructure — the Kirkuk-Ceyhan and TurkStream pipelines — as viable alternative energy corridors [6]. The Turkish business daily Dünya reported: 'Hürmüz krizi Türkiye'yi enerji koridorunda kilit ülke haline getirdi' (The Hormuz crisis has made Turkey a key country in the energy corridor) [6]. This dimension was entirely absent from European, East Asian, and Arabic coverage.

Russia dismissed the proposed mission as "unworkable." Russian Foreign Ministry spokeswoman Maria Zakharova accused France of seeking to create the appearance of participation in international processes rather than coordinating with the warring parties, including Iran [15]. Germany, however, signaled readiness to contribute to securing the Strait after a truce, according to Chancellor Friedrich Merz [16].

NATO allies had previously refused to join the US-led blockade of the Strait. Al-Monitor reported that Britain and France explicitly rejected President Trump's calls for allied participation in the blockade, preferring a separate diplomatic track [13]. NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte had briefed allies that Trump wanted commitments within days, but European nations chose to plan a post-conflict solution independently [13].

This report draws on 16 sources in 5 languages (English, French, Arabic, Chinese, Turkish, and Korean context via French reporting). No US government officials were quoted explaining Washington's absence from the summit or its position on the European-led initiative. No Gulf Arab state officials from Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, or Oman were directly quoted despite their geographic proximity to the Strait. No perspectives from oil-importing developing nations, shipping crews, tanker operators, or communities directly affected by the energy disruption were represented. No military officials from any country addressed the operational feasibility of the proposed mine-clearance mandate or naval escort logistics.

The summit concluded with participating nations signaling readiness to deploy the defensive mission once a stable ceasefire is achieved [12][1]. The next expected development is whether the April 21 ceasefire deadline holds, a condition Le Monde identified as prerequisite for any operational deployment [12]. Germany's stated willingness to contribute naval assets after a truce may shape the mission's scope [16], while Russia's dismissal and the continued absence of the United States and Iran from the framework leave the initiative's practical viability unresolved [15][13].
