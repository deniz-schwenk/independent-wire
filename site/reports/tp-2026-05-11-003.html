Hantavirus Cases Confirmed in US, France, and Germany as MV Hondius Evacuation Nears Completion

Health officials across multiple countries impose strict quarantine protocols while the WHO and epidemiologists stress the outbreak poses minimal risk to the general public.

This article draws on 19 sources in eight languages across 13 countries, offering broad geographic coverage of the multinational evacuation. However, no passengers, crew members, or their families are quoted anywhere, and the cruise operator Oceanwide Expeditions has not been heard from — meaning the people most directly affected and the company most directly responsible are both absent. The death toll also remains unsettled, with one WHO-attributed tally citing two confirmed deaths and one probable, while an earlier report cites three deaths among five cases, a discrepancy the article flags but cannot resolve.

New confirmed hantavirus cases among passengers repatriated from the MV Hondius cruise ship have been identified in the United States, France, and Germany, bringing the total number of confirmed infections to at least ten as the multinational evacuation operation from the Canary Islands enters its final phase [1][2]. The US Department of Health and Human Services reported that an American on a repatriation flight tested "mildly positive" for the virus and another showed mild symptoms, with both traveling in biocontainment units [1]. French Health Minister Stephanie Rist announced that a French passenger's condition was deteriorating and said a new decree would strengthen isolation measures for contact cases [1].

The outbreak, caused by the Andes strain of hantavirus — which carries a fatality rate estimated at 40 to 50 percent and is the only hantavirus strain known to transmit between humans — originated aboard the MV Hondius during an Antarctic expedition that transited Argentina, Chile, and Uruguay [1][2]. The World Health Organization has confirmed two deaths and one probable death, with four people hospitalized including one in intensive care in South Africa [1][14]; an earlier WHO tally cited by Anadolu Agency reported three deaths among five confirmed cases [2].

Governments receiving repatriated passengers have moved to impose some of the strictest quarantine measures seen since the COVID-19 pandemic. Rist stated, "What is key is to act at the start and break the virus transmission chains" [1]. French Prime Minister Sebastien Lecornu announced that a French passenger developed symptoms during the repatriation flight and was placed in strict isolation, adding that France would introduce isolation measures for contacts "to protect the general population" [3]. France's regional health authorities have mandated a 45-day isolation protocol with a 72-hour initial hospital stay for all returning passengers [10]. The Robert Koch Institute in Germany issued technical guidance for managing asymptomatic contacts exposed to the Andes strain [12]. The WHO has recommended a 42-day quarantine period for all cruise passengers [1].

Yet multiple officials and experts have simultaneously sought to contain public alarm. Robin May, chief scientific officer of the United Kingdom Health Security Agency, assessed the risk to the UK public as "extremely low" [1]. The CDC classified the outbreak as a Level 3 emergency response — its lowest emergency activation level — and stated that passengers would be monitored for approximately six weeks [2]. Harvard epidemiologist Bill Hanage argued that a wide hantavirus spread is unlikely despite the cruise ship deaths [18]. WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus stated explicitly, "This is not coronavirus," in what French media analysis described as a deliberate "yes-but" communication strategy designed to acknowledge the virus's severity while preventing pandemic-level panic [11].

The tension between aggressive containment measures and reassurances of low public risk has played out differently across national contexts. Ukrainian public health spokesperson Mykola Hanich noted that "In Ukraine, dozens of hantavirus infection cases are recorded annually, but this hantavirus is transmitted exclusively from rodents to humans" [4], drawing a clear distinction between the country's endemic hantavirus strains and the Andes variant's capacity for human-to-human transmission. Ukraine's Foreign Ministry confirmed that five Ukrainian crew members aboard the MV Hondius showed no signs of health deterioration [4].

The evacuation itself has been a logistically complex multinational operation. Spain's health minister reported that 94 passengers of 19 nationalities were evacuated on the first day, with the first to disembark being 14 Spanish nationals flown to Madrid for quarantine and testing [2]. Final flights to Australia and the Netherlands were scheduled for Monday, which would also carry passengers from countries without their own repatriation arrangements [1][2]. WHO Pandemic Preparedness Chief Maria Van Kerkhove announced that evacuation efforts were expected to conclude by Monday evening [3]. The Dutch government confirmed the repatriation of eight Dutch citizens to Eindhoven [13], while CBS News reported that 17 Americans were transferred to the University of Nebraska's biocontainment unit via Offutt Air Force Base [6]. Russian media reported a total of ten planned evacuation flights from Tenerife, divided between EU and non-EU destinations [16]. Argentina's Foreign Ministry coordinated with Dutch authorities for the repatriation of an Argentine passenger via a sanitized flight [8].

The decision to route the evacuation through Spain's Canary Islands generated friction between regional and central authorities. Canary Islands President Fernando Clavijo raised objections to bringing potentially infected passengers through the islands [2][3]. Spanish officials responded by dismissing concerns that infected Andean rodents aboard the vessel could reach the Canary coast, characterizing the probability as "zero" [2][3].

Some observers have questioned the visibility of key institutions. US media scrutiny focused on the CDC's perceived absence during the early outbreak stages, with STAT News asking why the agency had taken a back seat to the WHO despite American citizens being directly affected [7]. The CDC's acting director, Bhattacharya, stated that the risk to the US public was low while coordinating the deployment of response teams [7].

Several dimensions of the outbreak remain unexamined in available reporting. No passengers, crew members, or their families have been quoted about their direct experiences of quarantine, illness, or bereavement. Oceanwide Expeditions, the cruise operator, has not publicly addressed questions of liability or onboard pest control protocols. No independent virologists outside government agencies have provided on-the-record assessments of the Andes strain's transmission dynamics. South American health officials from Argentina, Chile, and Uruguay — where the virus is endemic and where initial exposure likely occurred — have offered limited public commentary, though Argentine health teams were dispatched to Ushuaia to capture rodents and collect environmental samples [9].

The evacuation operation is expected to conclude Monday evening, according to Van Kerkhove [3]. All repatriated passengers face quarantine periods of 42 to 45 days depending on their destination country [1][10], with ongoing clinical monitoring to detect any further cases among the approximately 150 people who were aboard the vessel [4].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”
