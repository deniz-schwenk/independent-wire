Spain's health minister announced that the MV Hondius, carrying 150 passengers of 23 nationalities and linked to five confirmed hantavirus infections and three deaths, will dock at Granadilla port in Tenerife for evacuation and quarantine at the Gómez Ulla military hospital [4][8]. The decision sets up a direct clash with the Canary Islands regional president, who objected to the plan and argued that repatriation should occur from Cape Verde instead [4].

The vessel has been at sea since passengers first fell ill with the Andes strain of hantavirus, which scientific experts say was likely acquired during excursions in South America before embarkation rather than from rodent infestation aboard the ship [12]. The World Health Organization has confirmed five cases and three deaths linked to the voyage and assessed the global risk as low [6][2].

**Quarantine and tracing across borders**

Contact tracing has now expanded to 12 countries. The UK Health Security Agency confirmed two cases among British nationals from the ship and is assessing a suspected infection on the remote island of Tristan da Cunha [1][5]. Asymptomatic British passengers returning home face a 45-day isolation protocol [5]. Singapore's Communicable Diseases Agency isolated two passengers who had disembarked at St. Helena for testing [9]. The US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention is monitoring individuals in Georgia, Arizona, California, and Texas, though it classified the outbreak as a Level 3 emergency — its lowest activation level [1][2].

The breadth of the precautionary response contrasts with the reassurance messaging from health authorities. Maria Van Kerkhove, the WHO's director of epidemic and pandemic management, stated: "This is not coronavirus, this is a very different virus" [1].

**Argentina's diplomatic row with the WHO**

The outbreak has also triggered a diplomatic dispute. Argentina's Ministry of Health accused the WHO of politicizing the MV Hondius infections to exert pressure on Argentina following its recent decision to withdraw from the organization [3]. Argentine officials separately reported that the country is experiencing a record surge of 101 hantavirus cases this season, which they attribute to climate-driven rodent population explosions and habitat destruction, while an independent expert characterized the cases as isolated incidents rather than a national outbreak [3][11]. The WHO has not publicly responded to the accusation [3].
