Ebola Cases in Eastern DRC Surpass 700 as Community Resistance Blocks Containment in Displacement Camps

Health workers have been expelled from camps housing tens of thousands of displaced people, while no licensed vaccine exists for the Bundibugyo strain driving the outbreak.

The article draws on 21 sources in six languages and carefully attributes divergent case counts to their respective dates and authorities, but its own voice occasionally colors the narrative — describing crowds as "angry" and labeling community distrust as the "central" barrier without attribution, which frames displaced populations primarily as obstacles to the response. Direct quotations come exclusively from international organizations and health workers, giving the text an institutional and humanitarian-operations perspective, while the communities at the center of the crisis are described but never heard in their own words.

Confirmed Ebola Bundibugyo cases in the Democratic Republic of the Congo have risen past 700, with the DRC government reporting 710 confirmed infections and 149 deaths as of mid-June, including 21 new cases registered within a single 24-hour period [4]. Al Jazeera reported a higher tally of 782 confirmed cases [1]. The outbreak, the country's 17th, is concentrated in Ituri Province but has spread across multiple provinces and into Uganda, where the World Health Organization recorded 19 confirmed cases as of 6 June — a figure that rose to 20 in a situation report dated 7 June [3] — roughly 70 percent of them Congolese nationals who crossed the border seeking care [13].

The case count has risen from 676 confirmed infections reported on 12 June [7] to the current figures in a matter of days. Africa CDC warned that the epidemic is "loin d'être maîtrisée" (far from being controlled) [7], and WHO declared a public health emergency of international concern in mid-May [4].

Community distrust of health authorities has become a central operational barrier. At the Kpangba displacement camp in Ituri, angry residents expelled health workers who arrived to investigate deaths, denied that Ebola was the cause, and refused safe burials and contact tracing [2][18]. Jean-Claude Lonzama, chief doctor for the Nizi health zone, stated: "Up to this day, we are not able to follow up on the contacts of these cases" [2]. He warned that no preventive measures have been put in place across 22 displacement sites housing around 81,124 people [2]. A 60-year-old woman who tested positive broke out of quarantine and could not be located [2].

The distrust has at times turned violent. An angry crowd set fire to an MSF treatment tent in Mongbwalu after authorities refused to release a body to the family; 18 suspected Ebola cases fled the facility, and health workers were attacked with stones during the same confrontation [19][20]. A safe burial team was attacked in South Kivu on 4 June [3]. Human Rights Watch called for prioritizing community engagement over reliance on security forces, arguing that enforcement-led approaches deepen hostility toward public health measures [9]. The Conversation, citing academic analysis, described how bans on large funeral wakes triggered confrontations and argued that transparency, community-driven action, and pre-outbreak trust-building are essential [5].

Swahili-language reporting by RFI documented how misinformation — "taarifa potofu" — is hampering the work of health workers and NGOs across eastern DRC, with the outbreak continuing to spread to new areas [11].

Armed conflict in Ituri has compounded the crisis. Attacks by armed groups have displaced more than 100,000 people into camps that lack food, health services, clean water, and hygiene materials, according to a ReliefWeb regional overview citing Save the Children [6]. UNICEF reported providing life-saving assistance to 100,000 children and families in the province [6]. Health workers in Ituri described patients dying for lack of transport to treatment centres: "Il y a des patients qui meurent faute de transport" (There are patients who die for lack of transport) [10]. Some cases were detected as early as February and March, pointing to delays in the response [10].

Frontline responders face direct personal risk. Three Red Cross volunteers in Ituri died after contracting Ebola during their work [12]. Several healthcare workers at a private hospital in Kampala, Uganda, became infected after treating a Congolese patient, an instance of nosocomial transmission [14]. Florent Uzzeni, MSF's emergency deputy for Switzerland coordinating the Ebola response in Ituri, stated: "Il reste encore beaucoup de travail à faire" (There is still much work to be done), citing detection delays and a lack of resources [17].

No licensed vaccine or treatment exists for the Bundibugyo strain. The existing Ervebo vaccine, developed for the Zaire strain of Ebola, lacks sufficient cross-protection data, and a WHO expert group decided not to deploy it, instead prioritizing two Bundibugyo-specific candidates for clinical trials [16]. MSF treatment centres in Ituri, North Kivu, and South Kivu are operating without either a vaccine or a proven therapeutic [8]. CEPI announced approximately $62 million in fast-track funding for three experimental Bundibugyo vaccine candidates, but clinical trials remain months away [15]. Moderna is developing one of those candidates and may receive up to $50 million of that CEPI envelope [4], and Gavi and the World Bank pandemic fund have pledged additional support [4].

The funding picture remains uneven. WHO and Africa CDC launched a joint $518 million response plan for Central Africa [21]. Yet the broader UN humanitarian appeal for the DRC is only 34 percent funded [21]. Uzzeni described persistent shortages of means and logistical failures that current funding has not resolved [17].

The WHO situation report as of 7 June recorded 550 laboratory-confirmed cases and 101 deaths in the DRC, with 229 new cases in the preceding week alone, and noted that contact follow-up coverage remains suboptimal [3]. The gap between that figure and the DRC government's 710-case count reported a week later reflects the pace of new infections. Uganda's Ministry of Health has implemented surveillance, contact tracing, and health-worker protection measures, but WHO assessed the risk in Uganda as high due to continued cross-border movement [13][14].

The next phase of the response hinges on whether community engagement can replace confrontation in displacement camps, and on whether experimental vaccines can move from laboratory to field before the outbreak spreads further. No timeline for the start of clinical trials has been publicly announced [15].

Perspectives — Positions

Position 1
Community distrust and resistance are the primary obstacles preventing effective Ebola containment in eastern DRC
Multiple sources document how populations in displacement camps and urban areas reject Ebola diagnoses, expel health workers, refuse safe burials and contact tracing, and in some cases violently attack treatment facilities. This distrust — rooted in misinformation, lack of transparent communication, and historical grievances — has left health authorities unable to implement basic containment measures across dozens of sites housing tens of thousands of displaced people.

Divergences

factual
Uganda case count: WHO Disease Outbreak News (src-013, data as of 6 June) reports 19 confirmed cases in Uganda, while the ReliefWeb WHO situation report (src-003, data as of 7 June) reports 20 confirmed cases including five healthcare workers.
Partially resolved: The corrected article now cites both figures with their respective dates, noting the count rose from 19 to 20 across the two reports.
factual
Total DRC case counts vary significantly across sources and dates: src-008 (MSF, 4 June) reports 381 confirmed cases; src-003 (WHO, 7 June) reports 550; src-007 (RFI/Africa CDC, 12 June) reports 676; src-004 (Tagesschau/DRC government, ~14 June) reports 710; src-001 (Al Jazeera, 14 June) reports 782.
Resolved: The article attributes each figure to its source and date, making clear these reflect different reporting dates and authorities rather than a single contested fact.
factual
Death toll figures also vary: src-007 (Africa CDC, 12 June) reports 136 deaths; src-004 (DRC government, ~14 June) and src-001 (Al Jazeera, 14 June) both report 149 deaths; src-003 (WHO, 7 June) reports 101 deaths.
Resolved: The article uses the most recent DRC government figure of 149 deaths with attribution, and the WHO 7 June figure is cited separately in context. The Africa CDC 136-death figure is not separately cited but is consistent with the chronological progression.
framing
The Mongbwalu tent fire and stone-throwing attack on health workers are reported as a single incident by src-019 (Euronews, 22 May) and src-020 (Cumhuriyet), but the original article presented them as two separate incidents.
Resolved: The corrected article merges these into a single described incident with both citations, removing the misleading 'separate incident' framing.
emphasis
German-language coverage (src-004) foregrounds international funding and vaccine development, while English and French sources emphasize community dynamics and operational barriers. The article integrates both dimensions.
Resolved: The article covers both the vaccine/funding dimension and the community resistance dimension across separate paragraphs.
omission
No voices from displaced communities, DRC government officials, or independent epidemiologists are directly quoted, as noted in missing_positions. The article relies on health workers, NGOs, and international organizations for all direct quotations.
Unresolved: The source base does not include direct quotes from displaced persons or DRC government ministers; this gap cannot be corrected from available sources.

What is missing

Voices missing

  • Displaced persons, camp residents, and family members of Ebola patients in the DRC, whose grievances and reasoning behind community resistance are mediated entirely through external observers
  • DRC government officials — ministers, provincial governors, or national Ebola response coordinators — explaining their strategy, resource allocation decisions, or response to community distrust
  • Independent epidemiologists, medical anthropologists, or public health researchers assessing the outbreak's trajectory, containment measures, and social dynamics of distrust
  • DRC or UN security forces operating in Ituri, despite armed conflict being identified as a key driver of displacement and a major complicating factor for the health response
  • Pharmaceutical company representatives — from Moderna or other vaccine developers — on timelines, trial design, or the feasibility of deploying experimental vaccines during an active outbreak
  • Congolese or broader African media outlets, meaning local journalistic framing, domestic political debate, and Congolese public opinion on the response are entirely absent
  • Ugandan sources, despite Uganda having recorded 20 cases including five healthcare workers, leaving the cross-border dimension and Uganda's own containment perspective uncovered
  • WHO officials or spokespeople, despite the organization's declaration of a PHEIC and its central coordination role
  • French-language sources, despite French being the DRC's official language and the primary language of its government communications and domestic media
  • Frontline healthcare workers — whether Congolese health staff or international responders such as MSF personnel — despite health worker safety and community confrontations being a major theme

Topics missing

  • Security and armed-conflict dynamics in detail — specifically the role of armed groups such as CODECO or other militias in Ituri — despite displacement from armed violence being identified as a key factor complicating the outbreak response
  • Ethical and logistical dimensions of deploying an unlicensed Bundibugyo-specific vaccine, including informed consent protocols and regulatory pathways, despite the absence of a licensed vaccine being central to the story's urgency

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
