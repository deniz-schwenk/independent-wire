A magnitude 7.5 earthquake struck off Japan's Sanriku coast at 4:53 p.m. local time (0753 GMT) on April 20, 2026, prompting the Japan Meteorological Agency (JMA) to issue major tsunami warnings for the Pacific coasts of Iwate, Aomori, and Hokkaido prefectures [1][5]. The epicentre was located approximately 100 kilometres east of Miyako, Iwate Prefecture, at a shallow depth of roughly 10 kilometres [5][6]. Some international sources reported the magnitude as 7.4 rather than 7.5 [6][7]. Seismic intensity reached upper 5 on Japan's scale in parts of Aomori and Iwate [4][6].

The JMA warned that tsunami waves of up to three metres could strike the affected coastline and urged residents to evacuate immediately to higher ground [1][5]. The agency's updated disaster prevention protocols, implemented in April 2026, classify such orders as Level 4 evacuation directives, with Level 3 orders triggering early evacuation for elderly and mobility-impaired residents [2]. In Niikappu, Hokkaido, 3,650 people from 2,024 households were evacuated within the first hours [4]. Tohoku Shinkansen bullet train services between Tokyo and Shin-Aomori were suspended [5][7].

The Pacific Tsunami Warning Center (PTWC) initially assessed the potential for dangerous tsunami waves across the wider Pacific region but subsequently determined that no destructive Pacific-wide tsunami was expected and that no threat existed to Hawaii [3]. The Japanese government established an emergency response countermeasure office to coordinate disaster relief, with Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi calling on residents in warning zones to evacuate to higher ground and safer locations [9].

Japanese-language sources framed the event as a live, unfolding emergency requiring immediate public action, emphasising evacuation protocols, warning levels, and real-time seismic data [1][2]. International English-language outlets treated the event as a developing news story, reporting on the magnitude, depth, and geographic parameters while noting the suspension of transport services [4][5][6][7]. This temporal and tonal split — urgent operational guidance versus after-the-fact news coverage — is a consistent pattern in earthquake reporting from Japan.

The humanitarian scale of the event drew varying emphasis across regions. Chinese-language and Indonesian-language reporting on the December 2025 precedent earthquake in the same region foregrounded the displacement of 90,000 residents and at least 30 injuries [10][11]; the LA Times reported 23 injuries for that same event [15], while Courthouse News cited 34 [9] — figures that reflect the fluid nature of casualty reporting in the immediate aftermath. German-language sources emphasised technical details such as focal depth (54 km in the December event), observed wave heights of 70 cm (also reported by Indonesian-language sources [11]), and a fire in Aomori prefecture [12][13]. Spanish-language coverage of the December 2025 event initially reported no victims and stressed the earthquake's geographic parameters — 80 km from Aomori at 50 km depth — framing it as a near-miss [14]. These divergent emphases illustrate how the same seismic profile produces different narratives depending on editorial priorities.

The nuclear safety dimension remains largely unaddressed in current reporting. The LA Times, covering the December 2025 precedent event in the same region, noted minor radioactive water spills at facilities without safety consequences [15]. No other source in the current dossier — Japanese, Chinese, European, or Southeast Asian — mentions nuclear plant impacts for either the December 2025 or April 2026 events, despite the Sanriku coast's proximity to the Higashidori and Rokkasho nuclear facilities in Aomori prefecture [15].

Divergence types

One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.

Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”

Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”
