Kyiv Mass Shooting Leaves Six Dead; Ukraine Classifies Attack as Terrorism Amid Competing Narratives Over Suspect's Identity

A 58-year-old Moscow-born man with Ukrainian citizenship opened fire in the Holosiivskyi district and took hostages in a supermarket before being killed by police, prompting divergent framing from Ukrainian, Western, and Russian media.

Six people were killed and at least 14 wounded on April 18, 2026, when a gunman opened fire on pedestrians in Kyiv's Holosiivskyi district before barricading himself inside a Velmart supermarket with hostages, according to Ukrainian and international reports [1][5][8]. Ukraine's Security Service (SBU) classified the attack as a terrorist act under Article 258 of the Criminal Code [2], and Prosecutor General Ruslan Kravchenko confirmed the terrorism investigation [6]. The suspect, identified as Dmytro Vasylchenkov, was killed by police special forces after a 40-minute standoff in which he refused to negotiate [10][11].

Vasylchenkov, born in Moscow in 1968, held Ukrainian citizenship and had lived in the Donetsk region before relocating to Kyiv's Demiïvka neighborhood [1][12]. According to UNN, he served in Ukraine's Armed Forces until 2004 and then returned to military service in 2022 [12]. President Volodymyr Zelenskyy stated the suspect had a criminal record and that the SBU was investigating the attack as terrorism [1][9]. Interior Minister Ihor Klymenko confirmed the weapon used was a legally registered carbine, with the permit renewed in December 2025 on the basis of a medical certificate whose origin authorities said they would investigate [13].

The attack began when Vasylchenkov set fire to his own apartment and then opened fire on passersby at close range [6][1]. Four people were killed on the street, and the gunman then entered the supermarket, where he took hostages [7]. One hostage was killed during the standoff, and a sixth victim — a 30-year-old woman — later died in hospital [5][12]. Among the dead were a 10-year-old boy and his father, killed on the street, and the boy's mother, who died of her injuries [12]. A 12-year-old child and a four-month-old baby were among the injured [1][14]. After negotiations failed, KORD special forces stormed the supermarket and killed Vasylchenkov; four remaining hostages were rescued [9][11].

The framing of the suspect's identity diverges sharply across media ecosystems. Ukrainian government sources and Western outlets emphasized Vasylchenkov's Russian birth, his criminal record, and the SBU's terrorism classification, situating the event within the broader context of the Russia-Ukraine conflict [1][2][8]. Zelenskyy referenced "multiple versions" of the motive under investigation but did not elaborate [1]. Nasha Niva, a Belarusian independent outlet, reported that Vasylchenkov had posted antisemitic and anti-Ukrainian content on social media [14].

Russian state-aligned media offered a starkly different narrative. Gazeta.ru reported that Vasylchenkov had been "forcibly mobilized" into the Ukrainian military and was a deserter, framing the attack as a consequence of Ukraine's coercive mobilization policies rather than external aggression [3]. As Moskovskij Komsomolets headlined: «Искали руку Кремля, а нашли бойца ВСУ» (They looked for the Kremlin's hand and found a soldier of the Armed Forces of Ukraine), presenting the terrorism classification as a politically motivated attempt to blame Russia that collapsed when the suspect's Ukrainian military service emerged [4]. Neither Russian outlet cited named sources for the claim of forced mobilization, and no official statement from Ukraine's Armed Forces confirming or denying the desertion claim was available in any source reviewed.

French-language coverage took a distinct approach. Le Monde foregrounded the operational timeline of the police assault and the hostage rescue, giving more weight to the tactical law enforcement dimension than to the geopolitical identity questions dominating Ukrainian and Russian reporting [5]. Zonebourse similarly emphasized the suspect's refusal to negotiate and the mechanics of the police response [10]. This contrasts with English-language outlets such as NPR and The Japan Times, which led with the terrorism classification and the suspect's Russian origins [1][8].

Unverified claims circulated in some media environments that Vasylchenkov may have held the rank of lieutenant colonel in the Russian Armed Forces and had connections to the Russian Airborne Forces (VDV), based on email addresses and social media posts attributed to him [12]. Ukrainian journalist Vitaliy Hlahola reported these details, but the Prosecutor General's office stated that information about possible involvement in Russian military structures had not been confirmed [12]. The preliminary_divergences note a separate claim — attributed to sources not included in this article's source pool — that the suspect held the rank of colonel in a specific Russian military unit; this detail was absent from all English, Ukrainian, French, and Russian sources reviewed and could not be verified.

The question of how Vasylchenkov obtained and retained a legal weapon permit despite a criminal record has drawn attention. Under Ukrainian law, individuals with criminal records are generally barred from holding firearms permits [13]. Klymenko stated that the permit was renewed in December 2025 and that the medical certificate used in the renewal process would be investigated [13]. No statements from gun control advocacy groups, victims' rights organizations, or civil liberties organizations were available in the sources reviewed.

Xinhua reported five confirmed dead and 15 injured — figures that differ from the six dead and at least 14 wounded reported by most Western and Ukrainian outlets, with the discrepancy in the death toll likely reflecting the timing of the sixth victim's death in hospital, while the difference in the wounded count may reflect varying reporting cutoffs [15]. The attack was the first mass shooting in Kyiv since Russia's full-scale invasion began in February 2022 [1].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”
