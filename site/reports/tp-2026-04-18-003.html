Thousands of Displaced Lebanese Return to Devastated South as Trump Declares Israeli Strikes 'Prohibited'

Mass civilian returns to rubble-strewn villages and a public U.S. directive against further Israeli bombing mark the second day of a fragile 10-day ceasefire, while both Israel and Hezbollah report ongoing violations.

Thousands of displaced Lebanese civilians began streaming back to southern Lebanon on April 18, 2026, the day after a U.S.-brokered 10-day ceasefire between Israel and Hezbollah took effect [1][2]. The returns came as U.S. President Donald Trump publicly declared that Israel is "prohibited" from bombing Lebanon any longer, writing "Enough is enough!" on Truth Social [3][11][12]. The statement introduced a new variable into an already unstable truce: a direct American claim of constraint over Israeli military operations, issued while both sides continued to report violations on the ground [5][8].

The ceasefire, which began on April 17, bars Israel from offensive military operations against Lebanese targets while preserving its right to self-defense against "planned, imminent, or ongoing attacks" [12]. Israel committed to a halt in offensive operations, while Lebanon pledged to prevent Hezbollah from launching attacks across the border [12]. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, however, refused to withdraw Israeli forces from a security buffer zone in southern Lebanon — described in German-language reporting as approximately 10 kilometers deep [9] — describing the truce as a strategic opportunity rather than a permanent cessation of operations [1][4]. The Israeli Defense Forces stated they remain on high alert and have continued striking Hezbollah targets they characterize as preventive measures against violations [8].

Trump's declaration that the United States has "prohibited" further Israeli strikes drew sharply different interpretations across global media. Spanish-language and Russian-language sources framed the statement as a definitive U.S. directive constraining Israel. La Razón reported Trump's language as a direct prohibition — "Donald Trump 'prohíbe' a Israel bombardear Líbano" (Donald Trump 'prohibits' Israel from bombing Lebanon) [4]. Meduza highlighted Trump's use of capital letters on social media: "Трамп пообещал, что Израиль больше не будет бомбить Ливан — потому что США им это ЗАПРЕТИЛИ" (Trump promised that Israel would no longer bomb Lebanon — because the USA PROHIBITED it) [3]. English-language sources, by contrast, emphasized the friction between Trump's public posture and Netanyahu's stated position. The Jerusalem Post reported that Israel "preserves right to self-defense" under the ceasefire terms [12]. i24NEWS noted that Trump acknowledged "disagreements" with Netanyahu but called him "a good partner" [11].

On the ground, the ceasefire has not stopped military activity. German outlet n-tv reported that Hezbollah maintains its "Finger am Abzug" (finger on the trigger), detailing retaliatory strikes against Israeli soldiers near the town of Khiam immediately after the ceasefire began [5]. Chinese outlet Ziniu News documented earlier skirmishes in which Hezbollah fired rockets at the Manara settlement, claiming retaliation for Israeli breaches of the truce [10]. Taiwan's Central News Agency reported that the IDF continues to strike Hezbollah targets despite the ceasefire's commencement, framing these as necessary to prevent violations [8]. The attribution of blame follows a clear pattern across language groups: Israeli military sources, reported through Taiwanese and English-language outlets, frame continued IDF strikes as preventive [8][12], while Arabic and Chinese sources frame the same Israeli military activity as the primary ceasefire violation provoking Hezbollah retaliation [2][10].

The humanitarian dimension of the returns dominated Arabic-language coverage. Oman Daily described displaced families willing to return "بأي ثمن ولو فوق الركام" (at any cost, even atop the rubble), reporting that the Qasmieh Bridge — a critical route to the south — had been destroyed [7]. Xinhua Arabic reported families gradually returning to the town of Marjayoun, while the Lebanese Army warned civilians against approaching border villages such as Ayta al-Shaab, which remain occupied by Israeli forces [2]. Western English and German sources gave less attention to these physical conditions, focusing instead on the political fragility of the 10-day timeline and the strategic calculations of the parties involved [1][9]. Deutschlandfunk reported that Hezbollah's disarmament remains Israel's central long-term objective, with the maintenance of a 10-kilometer security zone as an interim measure [9].

The safety of international peacekeepers adds another dimension largely absent from most coverage. The French Ministry for Europe and Foreign Affairs condemned attacks on UNIFIL peacekeepers and convoys by non-state armed groups, demanding full implementation of UN Security Council Resolution 1701, including Hezbollah's disarmament [6]. UN experts separately condemned Israel's April 8 bombing campaign in Lebanon — carried out after a prior ceasefire announcement — as "illegal aggression" constituting indiscriminate strikes against civilians [14]. The UN Secretary-General welcomed the April 17 ceasefire and urged all parties to respect it, stating there is "no military solution" to the conflict [15].
