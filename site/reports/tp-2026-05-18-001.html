Trump Warns Iran 'There Won't Be Anything Left' as Drone Strikes Hit Gulf Nuclear and Energy Sites

Tehran vows to break the US naval blockade and threatens to turn the Sea of Oman into America's 'cemetery,' while drone attacks near the UAE's Barakah nuclear plant and in Saudi airspace add a kinetic dimension to the stalled negotiations.

This article draws on 30 sources across 15 languages and 20 countries, providing unusually broad geographic coverage of the crisis. However, the discourse is dominated by state and military officials on all sides — no Iranian, Gulf-state, or Lebanese civilians, no shipping industry voices, no international legal scholars, and no independent nuclear experts are heard, despite these groups bearing or assessing the direct consequences of the escalation. Readers should also note that Iran's claim of having damaged a US vessel has not been independently verified, and a factual discrepancy about a key Iranian official's title remains unresolved between sources.

Donald Trump issued an ultimatum to Iran on his Truth Social account, writing that "For Iran, the Clock is Ticking, and they better get moving, FAST, or there won't be anything left of them" [1][2][5]. The warning came as drone strikes hit targets near the UAE's Barakah nuclear power plant and Saudi Arabia intercepted three drones entering from Iraqi airspace, marking a material escalation beyond the diplomatic impasse that has defined the conflict in recent weeks [3][6][13].

Trump's rhetoric built on a pattern of threats that have intensified since April, when he posted that "a whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again" [1][12]. In a Fox News interview, he accused Iran of reneging on agreements five times, stating that Tehran "accepts everything and then withdraws" [4]. US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth told Congress the Pentagon has plans to "intensify measures" and "reallocate resources" if necessary [4], while Republican Senator Lindsey Graham urged renewed strikes on Iran's energy infrastructure, calling it the country's "weak point" [7].

Iran responded with defiance across multiple channels. Mohsen Rezai, a member of Iran's Expediency Discernment Council and former IRGC commander, declared that Iran would "break this naval blockade" and warned that the Sea of Oman would become America's "cemetery" [8]. Rezai stated that Iranian missiles had "seriously damaged" one of three US vessels during a Strait of Hormuz operation, a claim that has not been independently verified [7][8]. Abolfazl Shakarchi, spokesperson for Iran's armed forces, warned the US would face "new, aggressive, and surprising scenarios" and "sink into a swamp of its own making" [1][8]. A former IRGC commander described the US naval blockade as an "act of war" [10].

The gap between the two sides' negotiating positions remained wide. Iranian foreign ministry spokesperson Esmail Baghaei described Tehran's proposals as "responsible" and "generous" [2]. Iran's demands, reported via the Tasnim news agency, include an end to the war on all fronts including Lebanon, the lifting of sanctions, the release of frozen assets, compensation for war damages, and recognition of Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz [2][14]. Washington's reported conditions, conveyed through the Fars news agency, require Iran to keep only one nuclear facility, transfer enriched uranium — reported by Fars as approximately 400 kg — to the United States [2][9][14], and accept a framework that Trump suggested could involve a 20-year suspension of Iran's nuclear program [2]. Fars reported that Washington refused to release even 25 percent of Iran's frozen assets or pay reparations [5]. Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf, Iran's parliament speaker and chief negotiator, insisted there was "no alternative but to accept the rights of the Iranian people as laid out in the 14-point proposal," calling any other approach "completely inconclusive" [3].

The drone strikes on Gulf targets introduced a new kinetic dimension. The UAE defense ministry reported that three drones entered from the country's western border; two were intercepted, while a third struck an electrical generator outside the inner perimeter of the Barakah nuclear power plant [6][19]. Emirati presidential adviser Anwar Gargash called the strike a "terrorist targeting" that "represents a dangerous escalation" and "violates all international laws and norms" [6]. UAE foreign minister Sheikh Abdullah bin Zayed Al Nahyan informed IAEA Director General Rafael Grossi of the details and asserted the UAE's "full right to respond" [6]. Grossi expressed "grave concern" and called for "maximum military restraint" near nuclear facilities [3][8]. The Abu Dhabi Media Office stated that no injuries occurred and there was no impact on radiological safety levels [3][8]; the IAEA separately noted that emergency diesel generators were supplying power to Barakah's unit 3 [8]. Saudi Arabia's defense ministry separately reported intercepting three drones from Iraqi airspace and warned it would take "necessary operational measures" in response [3][13].

Israeli media outlets KAN and Channel 12, as reported by the Turkish newspaper Hurriyet, indicated that the Israeli military had completed preparations for strikes on Iran's energy infrastructure and was coordinating with US CENTCOM [7][29]. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu told his cabinet that Israel was "holding territory, clearing territory, protecting Israel's communities" and vowed to eliminate every architect of the October 7 attack [3].

The effective blockade of the Strait of Hormuz continued to reverberate through global energy markets. China's foreign ministry stated that "shipping lanes should be reopened as soon as possible" [5]. South Korean Foreign Minister Cho Hyun stressed the importance of "establishing maritime security and safety in the region and the world" during a call with Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi [15]. Coverage from Brazil, Vietnam, India, Italy, and South Korea detailed the inflationary pressure on fuel costs and the vulnerability of import-dependent economies [26][27][30][25][24]. African nations faced divergent impacts, with net oil importers like Kenya absorbing higher costs while exporters like Nigeria benefited from price increases [22].

Diplomatic efforts continued on multiple tracks. Araghchi held calls with French and South Korean counterparts, blaming US and Israeli "aggressive actions" for regional insecurity [15]. Pakistan's Interior Minister Mohsin Naqvi arrived in Tehran as part of mediation efforts [3][28]. Russian President Vladimir Putin discussed the crisis with UAE President Mohamed bin Zayed Al Nahyan, emphasizing the importance of "political and diplomatic processes" for a compromise-based resolution [3][31].

Within the United States, former Congresswoman Marjorie Taylor Greene warned that deploying American troops to Iran would trigger a "political revolution," declaring "WE. ARE. DONE. We said no more foreign wars and we meant it" [16][7]. Analyst Adam Clements suggested that Trump's rhetoric may be aimed partly at domestic audiences, advising observers to watch whether senior officials echo the threats and whether military deployments actually increase [1]. NATO intelligence assessed that Iran retains at least 60 percent of its missile capacity [14].

Trump is scheduled to meet his national security team on Tuesday to discuss options, according to The Guardian [6]. Amnesty International has warned that threats to destroy civilian infrastructure constitute potential war crimes under international humanitarian law [21]. The ceasefire, which Trump himself described as being on "massive life support" [2][3], remains the only framework preventing a resumption of full-scale hostilities.

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
