Ukraine Launches One of Its Largest Drone Strikes on Moscow and Russian Regions, Killing at Least Seven

Kyiv calls the operation an entirely justified response to sustained Russian bombardment; Moscow denounces it as a mass terrorist attack on civilians.

May 18, 2026

This article draws on 27 sources in six languages across 11 countries, providing unusually broad geographic coverage, but no international humanitarian organizations, independent legal scholars, or US government officials are represented — a notable gap given documented civilian casualties on both sides and Washington's role as Ukraine's largest military supporter. The article generally handles competing Russian and Ukrainian framings with clear attribution, though it occasionally adopts evaluative language in its own voice — such as characterizing the operation as a 'sharp escalation' and 'large-scale strategic offensive' — that leans toward editorial judgment rather than neutral description. Readers should also note that only one civilian witness from the affected areas is quoted across the entire piece, leaving the human impact of strikes on both Russian and Ukrainian populations largely told through official statements rather than firsthand accounts.

Ukraine struck Moscow and more than a dozen Russian regions overnight on May 17 with what multiple outlets described as one of the largest drone barrages of the war, killing at least seven people across multiple regions and wounding more than twenty [2][5][7]. The operation marked a sharp escalation beyond the mutual ceasefire violations that followed the collapse of the Victory Day truce earlier this month, shifting from contested defensive postures to a large-scale strategic offensive reaching deep into Russian territory.

Russia's Defense Ministry stated that air defense systems intercepted and destroyed 556 Ukrainian drones overnight across multiple regions, with a further 30 neutralized after dawn [1][3]. By midday, the ministry said more than 1,000 drones had been downed in the preceding 24 hours [7]. Moscow Mayor Sergey Sobyanin described the raid as "massive" and reported that 127 unmanned aerial vehicles were destroyed over the capital in 24 hours — up from the 81 he cited for the overnight period alone — with 12 people wounded — mostly construction workers near the entrance to the Moscow Oil Refinery — though he said the plant's operations were not disrupted [11][10][1][7]. Moscow region Governor Andrei Vorobyov reported three deaths: a woman killed when a drone struck a private house in Khimki, and two people killed at a construction site in Pogorelki — reported variously as two men or a man and a woman across different sources [2][9][11]. India's Moscow embassy confirmed that one Indian national was among the dead and three others were injured [2][18][24].

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy framed the strikes as proportionate retaliation. "Наші відповіді на продовження Росією війни та її атаки на наші міста і громади є цілком виправданими" (Our responses to Russia's prolongation of the war and its attacks on our cities and communities are entirely justified), he stated, adding that Ukrainian drones had flown more than 500 kilometers from Ukrainian territory and overcome concentrated Russian air defenses around the capital [5][7]. "Мы четко говорим россиянам: их государство должно закончить свою войну" (We are clearly telling the Russians: their state must end its war), he said [10]. Ukraine's Defense Ministry declared that "the war is returning to where it came from" [9].

The Security Service of Ukraine (SBU) identified specific targets it said were struck: the Angstrem semiconductor plant, which supplies microchips to Russia's defense-industrial complex and is under US sanctions; the Moscow Oil Refinery; the Solnechnogorsk and Volodarskoye oil pumping stations; and air defense systems at the Belbek military airfield in occupied Crimea [10][16]. SBU head Евгений Хмара (Yevhenii Khmara) stated the strikes "имеют критическое значение для ослабления военного потенциала РФ" (are critically important for weakening Russia's military potential) [10]. A separate strike reportedly caused a fire at the Nevinnomyssk Azot fertilizer plant in Stavropol Krai, which Ukrainian officials linked to explosives production [16]. Stavropol Governor Vladimir Vladimirov confirmed the region came under drone attack but said air defenses "repelled" it [16].

Russia's Foreign Ministry spokesperson Maria Zakharova offered a starkly different characterization: "To the sound of Eurovision songs, the Kyiv regime, financed by the EU, carried out yet another mass terrorist attack" [7]. Russian state media framed the strikes as indiscriminate attacks on civilians intended to compensate for battlefield setbacks, with RT describing an escalation of Ukrainian "terrorist attacks" deep inside Russia since mid-March [11][18]. Ryazan region Governor Павел Малков (Pavel Malkov) reported that 99 drones attacked his region, damaging two residential buildings, killing four people including a child, and wounding 12 [10].

Konstantin, a 39-year-old resident of the Moscow suburb of Putilkovo, described the impact: "The hit was so powerful that it almost knocked me out [of bed], and I weigh a lot. I opened my window and saw smoke rising" [9]. He was the only Moscow-area civilian quoted across the reporting.

Not all Ukrainian analysts viewed the operation as optimally executed. Роман Свитан (Roman Svitan), a retired Ukrainian colonel, assessed that over 700 drones and various missiles were used but that most were shot down and the surviving hits did not strike the most significant targets. He argued the strike should have been launched on May 9 — Victory Day — for far greater psychological impact, and that future operations should concentrate on oil refineries to damage Russia's economy [10].

The drone war's spillover into NATO airspace added a further dimension. Lithuania's national crisis management centre reported that a suspected Ukrainian military drone was found crashed in the village of Samane, 40 kilometers from the Latvian border [5]. The Latvian army issued a drone alert along its border with Russia and scrambled NATO fighter jets on a Baltic air police mission after a drone briefly entered Latvian airspace [5][17][19]. These incidents followed an earlier episode on May 7 in which two Ukrainian drones fell in Latvia's Latgale region after being knocked off course by Russian electronic warfare, triggering the resignation of Latvia's defense minister Andris Sprūds and, according to some reports, contributing to the prime minister's resignation as well, and contributing to broader political fallout [17][33][28].

The context for the Ukrainian operation was a sustained Russian bombardment of Ukrainian cities. Zelenskyy stated that Russia had launched over 3,170 attack drones, more than 1,300 guided aerial bombs, and 74 missiles over the preceding week, killing 52 people and injuring 346 [21]. On the night following the Moscow strikes, Russian forces launched 524 drones and 22 missiles at central and southern Ukraine, with Dnipro as the primary target; a 24-storey residential building was hit, injuring at least 18 people including two children [8][13]. Dnipropetrovsk Governor Oleksandr Hanzha reported 26 wounded across six districts [14][20]. Strikes on Odesa damaged residential buildings, a school, and a kindergarten, injuring an 11-year-old boy and a 59-year-old man [22][4].

China's Foreign Ministry urged restraint from both sides without condemning either party, stating that Beijing's position on the conflict remained "consistent and clear" [23]. No US government reaction appeared in available reporting despite Washington being Ukraine's largest military supporter [3].

The following night, Russian air defenses reported shooting down only 50 Ukrainian drones — eleven times fewer than the previous day — with no damage or casualties reported [6]. Whether the lull signals a pause or a regrouping before further strikes remains unclear.

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”
