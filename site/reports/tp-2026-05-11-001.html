Trump Rejects Iran's Peace Proposal as 'Totally Unacceptable' While Oil Prices Surge Past $105 per Barrel

Pakistan-mediated negotiations stall as Tehran demands reparations and base withdrawals, Russia and China challenge the US naval posture in the Strait of Hormuz, and the collapse of maritime insurance markets forces governments into emergency backstop roles.

The article's own voice occasionally amplifies drama — phrases like "reverberating across continents" and "the widest gap yet" — where the substantial underlying data could speak for itself.

President Donald Trump publicly dismissed Iran's counter-proposal for ending hostilities as "TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE," a rejection delivered after the document reached Washington through Pakistani mediators [5][3]. The proposal, transmitted through Pakistani mediators, focused on ending the war on all fronts and securing navigation in the Strait of Hormuz [3]. Oil prices jumped above $105 per barrel following Trump's statement and renewed threats against Tehran, a roughly 40 percent increase triggered by Trump's threats [36].

The Islamabad talks had brought together high-level delegations — US Vice President JD Vance and senior adviser Jared Kushner on one side, Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf on the other — with Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif acting as intermediary [37]. Trump wrote on social media: "I have just read the response from Iran's so-called 'Representatives.' I don't like it — TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE!" [5]. A senior US official separately indicated that Washington may also pressure Beijing over purchases of Iranian oil during Trump's upcoming state visit to China, scheduled for May 13–15 [26]. Principal deputy White House press secretary Anna Kelly described the Beijing trip as one of "tremendous symbolic significance" focused on "rebalancing" the bilateral relationship [26].

Tehran's conditions, as outlined by a member of Iran's parliamentary National Security Commission, include war reparations, the withdrawal of US military bases from the region, and guarantees of Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz [34]. Iran International, a Persian-language outlet based in London, reported that Iran also demanded an end to the naval blockade and assurances against future attacks [33]. No direct statement from Iran's executive branch — the Supreme Leader, President, or Foreign Minister — appeared in available reporting, leaving the principal respondent's own characterization of the rejection largely absent.

The economic consequences of the standoff are reverberating across continents. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has collapsed the private war-risk insurance market for the waterway, prompting the US Development Finance Corporation to establish a $40 billion reinsurance backstop to keep tankers moving [40]. Le Monde estimated Iran's own losses at $270 billion, with two to four million jobs destroyed by the blockade [43]. El País reported that Latin American oil-importing economies such as Chile face severe inflation, while exporters like Brazil gain revenue — but all are operating with diminished fiscal room [44]. Asharq News noted the 40 percent price surge followed Trump's threats, compounding pressure on Gulf energy exports [36].

Russia and China offered a sharply different reading of the naval operations. Russian Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov stated that one of the goals of the US military operation was to seize control of oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz [39]. Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesperson Guo Jiakun expressed concern over the US interception of tankers and called for a ceasefire, while Beijing rejected Washington's "unilateral" sanctions targeting Iran's oil sector [38][26]. Both governments framed the root cause of the crisis as US-Israeli military actions rather than Iranian provocation [38][39].

Former Qatari Prime Minister Sheikh Hamad bin Jassim Al Thani argued in an Al Jazeera interview that Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu is exploiting the conflict to pursue a vision of a "Greater Israel," stating: "We are witnessing a major restructuring of the region" [4]. He described the weaponization of the Strait of Hormuz as the most dangerous outcome of the war and called for a unified Gulf defense pact — a "Gulf NATO" — to counter what he characterized as the disunity being exploited by external powers [4].

The Yemeni government directly blamed Iran for Houthi threats to navigation in the Bab el-Mandeb strait, framing the crisis as part of Tehran's broader regional agenda rather than an independent Yemeni conflict [35]. Asharq Al-Awsat reported that Yemeni officials tied the navigation threats to Iran's strategic leverage over global energy security [35].

European governments signaled caution about deeper military involvement. Italian Defense Minister Guido Crosetto drew a distinction between repositioning naval assets under existing missions and launching a direct Hormuz mission, stating: "Un conto è far avvicinare le navi e un altro è dirigerle direttamente verso Hormuz" (Moving ships closer is one thing; directing them straight to Hormuz is another) [2]. Crosetto said a direct mission would require a ceasefire, a new legal framework, and explicit parliamentary authorization, and that he would discuss options with Parliament starting Wednesday [2].

The broader geopolitical landscape adds further complexity. Trump's upcoming Beijing visit, the first US presidential state visit to China in nearly nine years, was itself postponed once before due to the Iran war [26]. US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer said Washington was seeking to preserve stability in bilateral ties while securing continued access to Chinese rare earth minerals [26]. Meanwhile, Anadolu Agency reported that Israeli strikes in Lebanon continue despite the ceasefire framework, and that the strait remains closed to normal commercial traffic [5].

The stalemate leaves Pakistan's mediation effort as the only active diplomatic channel between Washington and Tehran. With Trump's rejection now public and Iran's legislative demands publicly staked out, the next round of talks — if one materializes — faces the widest gap yet between the two sides' stated positions [3][34]. The World Economic Forum analysis warned that as long as the strait remains contested, governments will continue to function as insurers of last resort for global shipping, a role that strains public finances worldwide [40].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia

affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.

Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
