President Donald Trump dismissed Iran's response to the latest US peace proposal as "TOTALLY UNACCEPTABLE" on his Truth Social platform, triggering a 4.1 percent jump in Brent crude oil prices to $105.50 per barrel and placing global markets in risk-aversion mode [167][2]. The rejection, delivered on the 73rd day of the US-Israeli war on Iran, came hours after Pakistan confirmed it had received Tehran's counterproposal and transmitted it to Washington [6][28]. Trump warned that if Iran did not agree to a deal, "the bombing starts" at a higher level and intensity [159].

Iran's counterproposal, delivered through Pakistani mediators, demanded an immediate end to the war on all fronts — including Lebanon — a halt to the US naval blockade, the lifting of sanctions, the release of frozen Iranian assets, and recognition of Iranian sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz [23][15]. Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei described the offer as "legitimate" and "generous," stating: "We did not demand any concessions. Our demand is legitimate: demanding an end to the war, lifting the blockade and piracy, and releasing Iranian assets that have been unjustly frozen" [2]. Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian said Iran would "never bow down to the enemy" and that dialogue did not mean surrender [23][496].

Washington's position centers on the physical removal of Iran's enriched uranium stockpile and the dismantlement of its nuclear infrastructure. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu stated on CBS News that the war is "not over because there's still nuclear material, enriched uranium, that has to be taken out of Iran" [8][159]. Trump separately threatened to "blow them up" if anyone approached Iran's nuclear materials site, which he said was under heavy US surveillance via Space Force [12]. Andrea Dessi, an analyst at the American University of Rome, observed that "both sides continuously stick to rather maximalist positions," with the real casualties being the international economy and Gulf populations [2].

The diplomatic deadlock has sharpened a parallel confrontation over the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 20 percent of the world's oil supply normally transits. Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi warned that any French or British naval deployment near the strait would be met with a "decisive and immediate response" [11]. A top adviser to Iran's Supreme Leader, Mohammad Mokhber, compared the strait's strategic value to "an atomic bomb" [306][314]. Iran's military announced a new legal regime requiring all vessels to coordinate passage with Tehran, warning of "severe consequences" for non-compliance [159].

The United Kingdom and France are co-chairing a meeting of defense ministers from more than 40 nations to plan the military restoration of shipping through the strait [196]. UK Defence Secretary John Healey confirmed the deployment of HMS Dragon to the region, stating: "This government will not stand by when instability drives up costs for British families and businesses" [196]. French President Emmanuel Macron sought to defuse tensions by clarifying that France had "never envisaged" a naval deployment but rather a security mission "co-ordinated with Iran" [9][613]. Italian Defense Minister Guido Crosetto drew a legal distinction between repositioning assets closer to the region and launching a dedicated Hormuz mission, which he said would require a ceasefire, a legal framework, and parliamentary approval [228].

The economic ripple effects are reaching far beyond the Gulf. The International Energy Agency's executive director, Fatih Birol, stated that the loss of oil supply has reached 14 million barrels per day — "mayor que todas las crisis energéticas que se han presentado en la historia" (greater than all energy crises that have occurred in history) [439]. Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi urged citizens to work from home, reduce fuel consumption, and postpone gold purchases for a year to conserve foreign exchange, prompting opposition leader Rahul Gandhi to call the appeal "proof of failure" [531][522]. South Korea announced a second round of cash assistance to approximately 36 million people — the bottom 70 percent of income earners — to ease financial strains from rising fuel prices [125], while South Korean Finance Minister Koo Yun-cheol confirmed temporary fuel price caps would remain in place until the Middle East situation stabilizes [91].

Saudi Arabia has positioned itself at a deliberate distance from both Washington and Tehran. Saudi Foreign Minister Faisal bin Farhan Al Saud declared that "the era of relying on the United States has ended," while Riyadh denied the US use of its airspace and bases for the Hormuz escort operation [306][611]. Analyst Adel Bakawan of the Institut européen d'études du Moyen-Orient et d'Afrique du Nord stated that Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman "a refusé catégoriquement d'adopter une approche offensive contre la République islamique d'Iran" (categorically refused to adopt an offensive approach against Iran), describing Saudi Arabia as building a "Saudi nationalism" independent of both Western and Eastern alignment [611].

South Korea condemned a confirmed strike on the HMM-operated cargo vessel Namu in the Strait of Hormuz, with National Security Advisor Wi Sung-lac stating that attacks on civilian vessels "can neither be justified nor tolerated" [94]. An investigation concluded that two unidentified airborne objects caused the explosion [154]. The opposition People Power Party accused the government of downplaying an Iranian attack, while the ruling Democratic Party warned against hasty conclusions ahead of local elections [93]. Seoul is now reviewing possible participation in the US-led Maritime Freedom Construct [151].

Former Qatari Prime Minister Sheikh Hamad bin Jassim Al Thani framed the entire conflict as the culmination of a decades-old Israeli agenda to reshape the Middle East, warning that Iran's weaponization of the Strait of Hormuz is the most dangerous consequence [1]. Qatari Prime Minister Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al Thani told Iran's foreign minister that using the strait as a pressure tool would only deepen the crisis and that freedom of maritime navigation is "nicht verhandelbar" (non-negotiable) [270].

Attention now turns to the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing from May 13 to 15, which analysts describe as the most likely venue for concrete progress on the Iran crisis. Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesperson Guo Jiankun stated that Beijing is willing to inject "greater stability" into the relationship [397]. Patricia M. Kim of the Brookings Institution predicted the most likely outcome is an extension of the current US-China trade truce, with Iran as the issue carrying the greatest potential for results [486]. The summit was postponed from March due to the war itself [305].

About these labels

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”
