Ukraine Launches Mass Drone Strikes Across Russia on Opening Day of St. Petersburg Economic Forum

More than 350 drones targeted energy infrastructure, a naval base, and an arms factory in at least 15 Russian regions as black smoke rose over Putin's flagship international gathering.

The article draws on 18 sources across multiple languages and gives space to Ukrainian, Russian, and Western voices, but its own editorial framing leans toward the Ukrainian strategic narrative — characterizing the strikes as "a new phase" and "a blow to the Kremlin's projection of control" without attributing those judgments. A handful of loaded terms in the article's own voice, such as labeling an exhibition a "propaganda display" and a regional leader "Kremlin-installed," embed editorial judgments that go beyond what neutral description requires. Factual discrepancies in casualty figures and drone interception counts are transparently flagged with ranges and competing attributions, which is a notable strength of the reporting.

Ukrainian drones struck energy and military targets across at least 15 Russian regions overnight on June 3, with the St. Petersburg Oil Terminal and the Kronstadt naval base among the sites hit hours before the opening of the St. Petersburg International Economic Forum [2][3][5]. Russia's Defense Ministry stated that 354 drones were intercepted [15], while Ukrainian officials confirmed strikes on the oil terminal, the corvette Boikiy at Kronstadt, and an arms factory in Tambov [7][10]. Black smoke from the burning oil terminal was visible from the forum venue [3][5]. The simultaneous targeting of multiple cities during Russia's premier economic event marked a new phase in Ukraine's long-range drone campaign, which had previously struck the same oil terminal days earlier.

President Volodymyr Zelensky described the attacks as part of a systematic plan. "Ukraine's plan for long-range sanctions is being implemented exactly as needed to bring peace closer," he stated, noting that the targets lay almost 1,100 kilometers from Ukraine's border [2][5][12]. Robert Brovdi, commander of Ukraine's Unmanned Systems Forces, said his units tracked and set fire to the corvette Boikiy, which he described as a vessel that "accompanied the shadow oil fleet — a real workhorse" escorting Russian tankers through the English Channel [7]. Sergiy Sternenko, an adviser to the Ukrainian Defense Ministry, wrote that "the Petersburg forum is opening with a nice plume of black smoke in the background after Ukrainian strikes" [3][5]. BBC News Україна reported that the oil terminal was the 20th Russian oil facility struck in 33 days [16].

Russian officials emphasized effective air defenses and limited casualties. Governor Alexander Beglov of St. Petersburg stated that several facilities on the city's outskirts were hit and buildings damaged, but reported no deaths [3][10]. Governor Alexander Drozdenko of Leningrad Oblast reported 59 drones shot down over his region [4][15], though the Kyiv Independent cited a figure of 50 [7]. Novaya Gazeta Europe noted that Beglov's statements did not mention the oil terminal attack [9]. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov stated: "Our responses will be systemic in nature" [2]. Flights at Pulkovo Airport were suspended for several hours, and mobile internet service in St. Petersburg was shut down during the attack [9].

The timing of the strikes drew attention from Western analysts and media as a blow to the Kremlin's projection of control. John Foreman, a former UK defense attaché to Moscow and Kyiv, stated: "It is exceptionally humiliating for Putin's home city to be attacked on the opening day of St. Petersburg International Economic Forum" [7]. The New York Times described the attacks as part of Kyiv's long-range campaign to inflict economic damage and highlight vulnerabilities [13]. Japan Today reported that the Institute for the Study of War assessed that "Putin is using massive strike packages against Kyiv in an effort to break Ukraine's will to fight as well as to disguise his weakness" [6].

Analysts linked the drone campaign to a broader shift on the battlefield. John Helin, co-founder of the Black Bird Group, stated that Russian territorial gains slowed to 82 square kilometers in May, adding: "If the Russians can't find ways to pick up momentum significantly, the goal of capturing Donbas this year is slipping out of their reach fast" [6]. NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte, visiting Kyiv on the same day, stated: "Russia's recklessness is not new. But as Ukraine continues to stand strong, to innovate and to make battlefield gains, Russia is increasingly desperate" [5][7]. Mathieu Boulègue of the Center for European Policy Analysis said Western sanctions were "really slowly, I think, changing the cost-benefit calculus of the Kremlin" [6].

A separate Ukrainian drone strike hit a passenger bus traveling from Moscow to Simferopol in occupied Yenakiieve in the Donetsk region, killing seven or eight civilians and wounding ten or eleven, according to varying reports [8][9][12][27]. Denis Pushilin, the Kremlin-installed leader of the Donetsk region, called the attack an act of "unprecedented, inhumane aggression" [10][4]. Russia's Investigative Committee opened a terrorism case [10]. At least two Emergency Situations Ministry employees were killed while responding to a drone-caused fire in Smolensk region, with one source reporting three deaths [8][9][12]. TF1 Info and Tagesschau both reported the bus strike casualties [27][10].

Inside the forum venue, the attacks were largely unacknowledged. Novaya Gazeta Europe reported that attendees at the Expoforum avoided discussing the drone strikes, even as smoke columns were visible outside [11]. The forum featured exhibitions including Chinese-branded Volga cars, robot demonstrations, and an RT propaganda display with plaster masks of Western politicians; lunch prices had risen 41 percent to 7,900 rubles [11]. Moscow's economy envoy Kirill Dmitriev described the gathering as one of "sovereign countries," stating: "The countries of the global south are actively moving toward partnership with Russia and will be strongly represented" [5].

On the streets of St. Petersburg, El País interviewed residents who described a different atmosphere. A resident identified as Anastasiya said: "Oí cómo llegaban los drones. Cómo los derribaban y explotaban" (I heard how the drones arrived. How they were shot down and exploded) [4]. A barbershop worker named Stanislav expressed pessimism: "Esto probablemente no terminará nunca" (This will probably never end), adding that he missed the time when he could travel freely to Finland [4]. A woman named María cited the Bible's promise that God would end all wars [4].

Tagesschau reported that German government circles see a window for European-led talks with Russia, with the E3 format of Germany, France, and Britain potentially playing a role, while US Secretary of State Marco Rubio signaled that others are welcome to try to end the war [12]. Brigadier General Andriy Biletsky, commander of Ukraine's Third Army Corps, stated that Ukraine has a six-month opportunity to strengthen its hand for peace talks [6].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
