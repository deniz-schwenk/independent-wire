US-Saudi Nuclear Cooperation Agreement Conditioned on Abraham Accords Normalization

US officials insist the deal upholds nonproliferation standards; Democratic lawmakers, nonproliferation experts, and Israeli security figures warn of enrichment risks and a regional arms race.

The United States and Saudi Arabia signed a civilian nuclear cooperation agreement on July 22, 2026, with the US Energy Department announcing the deal alongside a bilateral safeguards pact [2][6]. President Donald Trump stated the following day that the agreement "will be approved, but is totally subject to Saudi Arabia joining the very respected and successful Abraham Accords" [20]. White House spokesperson Karoline Leavitt said the deal is "contingent" on Saudi accession to the accords and that "if they don't join the Abraham Accords, the deal is off" [3][11].

The conditioning marked a shift from the initial announcement, which did not include a normalization requirement [9]. Saudi Arabia has maintained that normalization with Israel requires a framework for Palestinian statehood [17][22]. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu welcomed Trump's condition, calling Saudi Arabia's joining of the Abraham Accords "a historic leap forward for peace in the Middle East" and attributing the possibility to joint US-Israeli military action against Iran [9][22].

US Energy Secretary Chris Wright stated the agreements "uphold the highest standards of nuclear safety and nonproliferation, while relying on the world's best nuclear technology and scientists, designed right here in the United States" [4][5][7]. Secretary of State Marco Rubio said "any agreement that we are going to make with any country in the world on civil nuclear energy is going to be one that will have safeguards in place to ensure that it cannot be turned into a weapons program" [3][5]. The US Energy Department described the agreements as laying "the legal foundation for a decades-long, multi-billion-dollar partnership that advances several priority economic and strategic objectives, including nuclear nonproliferation" [6][7].

Democratic lawmakers, nonproliferation experts, and arms-control organizations disputed that assessment. Senator Edward Markey said the deal would "make us all less safe," accusing the administration of "allowing Saudi Arabia, a belligerent and authoritarian nation, to develop nuclear weapons technologies while starting a war with Iran under the guise of preventing an Iranian nuclear bomb" [6]. Senator Chris Murphy said the agreement "will set off a nuclear race in the region, further disincentivizing Iran from limiting its own program" [6]. Senator Bernie Sanders called it "absurd" that Trump claims his war on Iran is about stopping enrichment while letting Saudi Arabia "do exactly that" [5]. Congressman Ro Khanna described the deal as "a giveaway to the Saudis" that will "fuel further ambitions from Iran" [21][29]. The Arms Control Association stated the agreement "compromises essential nonproliferation guardrails" and "reverses 50 years of US leadership in the global nonproliferation architecture" [38].

The deal reportedly allows Saudi Arabia to potentially enrich uranium after a joint study [14][16] and does not include the so-called "gold standard" prohibiting enrichment and reprocessing, nor the IAEA Additional Protocol permitting unannounced inspections [6][8][24]. Trump, however, stated there would be "no enrichment of material" [3][9], and Energy Secretary Wright said the preliminary agreement was "not about enrichment" [24], contradicting reports about the deal's terms. The 2009 US-UAE agreement included both the gold standard and the Additional Protocol [23][24]. Rosemary Kelanic, Director of the Middle East programme at Defense Priorities, said "the US has never done that before. We have never helped another country enrich on their own soil" [4]. Matthew Kroenig of the Atlantic Council said "it would be a mistake to allow Saudi Arabia the capability for enrichment" [5][7].

Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman told CBS News in 2018 that "without a doubt if Iran developed a nuclear bomb, we will follow suit as soon as possible" [4][24][28]. The Saudi government framed the agreement as an extension of existing energy cooperation supporting diversification and incorporating "the highest international standards of nuclear safety, protection, and non-proliferation" [9]. Saudi state-aligned media presented the deal as a "historic" and "strategic partnership" aligned with Vision 2030, emphasizing economic scale and omitting discussion of enrichment risk or regional arms-race concerns [37].

Israeli security figures raised concerns. An unnamed senior Israeli official called enrichment on Saudi soil "a bright red line" and "a school for developing nuclear weapons" [35]. Former Israeli Defense Minister Avigdor Lieberman said "a civilian nuclear program in Saudi Arabia [will] lead to a mad arms race across the entire Middle East" [21] and warned that "every military nuclear program begins with a civilian one" [23]. Retired Israeli diplomat Jeremy Issacharoff described the agreement as "extremely dangerous" and indicative of Israel's diminishing capacity to influence US national security policy [12]. Israeli Economy Minister Nir Barkat offered a contrasting view, saying Israel "can manage it" if Saudi Arabia had nuclear power for peaceful means [4].

Analysts framed the agreement as securing a decades-long role for American companies while denying Riyadh's nuclear business to Russian and Chinese suppliers [5][7]. Jennifer T. Gordon of the Atlantic Council said "Saudi Arabia has made it clear that it will purchase nuclear energy technologies and begin a civil nuclear program; the only question has been whether the kingdom would choose to work with the US and its allies, or whether it would turn to Russia or China" [5][7]. Yusuf Can of Amena Strategies said "countries in the Middle East want to develop their nuclear infrastructure and they're going to do it even if the US is not involved" [21][29]. Rubio stated it is preferable for countries to pursue civilian nuclear programs with the US rather than another country [19].

Kremlin spokesperson Dmitry Peskov stated that Russia recognizes the right of any state, including Saudi Arabia and Iran, to peaceful nuclear energy [40]. A Pakistani commentator argued that Saudi Arabia already enjoys a nuclear umbrella through its defense relationship with Pakistan and that the deal primarily serves as a domestic political bailout for the Trump administration [43]. The IAEA stated it is aware of the plan but has not yet received a formal request to oversee inspections, adding it looks forward to working with both countries to ensure implementation of measures [20].

The deal will undergo congressional review under Section 123 of the US Atomic Energy Act [3][24]. Democratic lawmakers including Senators Richard Blumenthal and Mark Kelly and Congressman Brad Sherman expressed opposition or called for hearings, while Republican senators Jim Risch, James Lankford, and Mike Rounds offered conditional support pending review of the details [32]. The IAEA is awaiting a formal request before it can verify or oversee implementation [20].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”
