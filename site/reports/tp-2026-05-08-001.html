US and Iranian Forces Exchange Fire in Strait of Hormuz as Both Sides Dispute Who Broke the Ceasefire

Trump calls the naval clash a 'love tap' and insists the April 8 truce holds; Iran says it responded to a US strike on an oil tanker and civilian areas on Qeshm Island.

This article draws on 32 sources across 13 countries and 12 languages, giving it broad geographic reach, though no voices from affected civilians—Iranian residents of struck areas, Emirati residents sheltering from missiles, or maritime workers on disrupted vessels—appear anywhere, leaving the human cost entirely unrepresented. The competing US and Iranian accounts of who fired first and whether US ships sustained damage remain unresolved, and the article transparently flags this rather than adjudicating it. A few instances of editorializing language in the article's own voice—such as calling the ceasefire 'fragile' and describing diplomacy as 'stalled'—embed judgments that could be replaced with more neutral phrasing or attributed to named analysts.

Three US Navy destroyers — the USS Truxtun, USS Rafael Peralta, and USS Mason — exchanged fire with Iranian missiles, drones, and small boats while transiting the Strait of Hormuz, according to US Central Command [13]. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Navy confirmed it launched a combined ballistic and cruise missile operation against the warships, stating the attack was a response to a prior US strike on an Iranian oil tanker and civilian infrastructure on Qeshm Island [12][22]. The engagement, on day 70 of the conflict, sent Brent crude surging 7.5 percent to above $101 per barrel and deepened uncertainty over the fragile April 8 ceasefire [6].

President Donald Trump described the clash as "just a love tap" and insisted the ceasefire remained in effect, while warning Tehran to "better sign agreement fast" or face further military consequences [11][5]. CENTCOM said its forces "eliminated all inbound threats" and conducted "self-defense strikes" against Iranian missile sites and command nodes without sustaining damage [13]. Secretary of State Marco Rubio stated the United States was acting in self-defense [2]. The Pentagon maintained that the ceasefire holds despite more than ten Iranian attacks on US forces since the truce began [21].

Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya Central Headquarters accused the United States of violating the ceasefire first by targeting the Iranian tanker and striking civilian areas, and said the IRGC operation inflicted "significant damage" on the US vessels [2][6]. Iranian state outlets Tasnim and IRIB reported that the destroyers were forced to retreat from the strait toward the Sea of Oman [12]. Iran's Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf mocked diplomatic progress, saying "Operation Trust Me Bro failed" [1]. At the United Nations, Iran's representative Amir Saeid Iravani denounced a US-Bahrain draft Security Council resolution as an attempt to "legitimize the unlawful action of the United States against Iran in the Persian Gulf and the Strait of Hormuz, including its illegal maritime blockade" [14].

The competing accounts of who fired first and who sustained damage remain unresolved. Trita Parsi, executive vice president of the Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft, said Iran fears the US has constructed a framework in which "if the US decides to shoot, then that is not, in and of itself, a violation of the ceasefire" [2]. Ali Vaez, director of the Iran Project at the International Crisis Group, described the dynamic as one in which "the US is trying to loosen Iran's chokehold upon the Strait of Hormuz while Iran remains determined to push back" [2]. Political scientist Chris Featherstone of the University of York said the violence "could be seen as an escalation" but also demonstrates "the volatility of this conflict," suggesting both sides may use it to increase leverage in peace talks [2].

Diplomatic efforts remain stalled over a 14-point US peace proposal that requires Iran to halt uranium enrichment for 12 years and surrender its 440 kilograms of 60-percent enriched uranium [1]. Iranian lawmaker Ebrahim Rezaei called the plan "more of an American wish-list than a reality" [1]. Vaez argued that "both sides would either have to make painful concessions or leave the main areas of disagreement vague if they are to finalise a framework understanding" [1]. Pakistan continues to serve as an intermediary, having transmitted the proposal between Washington and Tehran [28].

Russia warned the UN Security Council against "pushing through one-sided and confrontational draft resolutions," saying such moves risk "a new wave of escalation," and reiterated its support for Pakistani mediation [10]. Moscow blamed the underlying crisis on "unprovoked aggression by the US and Israel" [10].

The fallout extended well beyond the immediate combatants. The UAE Ministry of Defence reported intercepting two ballistic missiles and three drones launched from Iran, bringing its cumulative total to 551 ballistic missiles, 29 cruise missiles, and 2,263 drones engaged since the start of the conflict [15]. The UAE's National Emergency Crisis and Disaster Management Authority told residents to seek shelter during the engagement [9]. France's President Emmanuel Macron condemned Iranian strikes on Emirati civilian infrastructure as "unjustified" [4].

At the 48th ASEAN summit in the Philippines, Southeast Asian leaders confronted the energy crisis head-on. Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. said the region is "in a limbo situation about how long it is going to last" and noted that 80 percent of crude transiting the Strait of Hormuz before the war was destined for Asian markets [7][8]. The bloc is preparing a joint statement calling for the immediate reopening of the strait and the establishment of a regional energy-sharing agreement [3]. Vietnam's limited oil reserves and broader food-supply vulnerabilities were highlighted as particular concerns [25].

China's foreign ministry spokesperson Lin Jian expressed concern after an oil tanker carrying Chinese crew members was attacked in the strait, calling for the restoration of "smooth passage" without assigning blame to either belligerent [20][24]. Brent crude has risen 40 percent since the war began, amid a 14.5-million-barrel daily production shortfall [6]. Toyota forecast a 20-percent decline in annual operating income to 3.0 trillion yen, citing supply-chain uncertainties linked to the conflict [18]. A Deloitte Korea analysis projected a 0.3-percent drop in South Korean GDP growth if oil remains at $100 per barrel [31]. Times Now Hindi reported that India faces rising fertilizer costs and GDP pressure from oil prices reaching $125 per barrel [26].

The Iranian army separately seized the oil tanker Ocean Koi in the Sea of Oman, alleging the vessel attempted to disrupt Iran's oil exports [17]. Euronews reported that the US had earlier targeted the rudder of the Iranian tanker Hasna in the Gulf of Oman [29]. Global shipping remains at a standstill [6].

No formal talks between Washington and Tehran are currently scheduled. Trump has threatened further "pain" if Iran does not agree to terms [5], while Iran's leadership has signaled it will not accept the current proposal without fundamental changes [1]. The next diplomatic test is expected at the Security Council, where the US-Bahrain draft resolution faces Russian opposition [10][14].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
