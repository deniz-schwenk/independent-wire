US and Iranian Forces Exchange Fire in Strait of Hormuz as Both Sides Claim Ceasefire Remains in Effect

Washington calls the engagement a 'love tap' in self-defense; Tehran says it retaliated after US strikes on an oil tanker and civilian areas on Qeshm Island.

This article draws on 32 sources across 10 languages and 13 countries, giving it unusually broad geographic reach, though no voices from Gulf Arab energy exporters like Saudi Arabia, stranded maritime crews, or Iranian civilians on Qeshm Island — all directly affected parties — are represented. The two core factual disputes (who fired first and whether US ships were damaged) are presented with clear attribution to each side but remain unresolved by independent evidence. The article's own language is largely restrained, with only occasional editorial characterizations such as calling the diplomatic situation "fractured" or the strait closure "near-total" without sourced support for those descriptors.

Three US Navy guided-missile destroyers exchanged fire with Iranian forces while transiting the Strait of Hormuz on May 8, with each side accusing the other of initiating hostilities during what both governments described as an ongoing ceasefire [2][13]. US Central Command stated that the USS Truxtun, USS Rafael Peralta, and USS Mason came under "unprovoked" attack from Iranian missiles, drones, and small boats, and that US forces responded with self-defense strikes against Iranian military facilities [13]. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Navy confirmed launching a "large-scale and precise combined operation" against the destroyers, stating it was retaliating for a US strike on an Iranian oil tanker near Jask port and attacks on civilian infrastructure on Qeshm Island [12][22].

President Donald Trump told reporters the ceasefire was still in effect and characterized the US response as a "love tap," adding: "They trifled with us today. We blew them away" [2][11]. Trump stated that the three destroyers sustained no damage while Iranian attackers were "completely destroyed along with numerous small boats" [11]. He warned Tehran to "better sign agreement fast" or face further consequences [5]. Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya Central Headquarters offered a directly contradictory account, stating that US vessels sustained "significant damage" and retreated after the Iranian strikes [2][12].

The competing narratives extend to the question of who violated the truce. CENTCOM described the Iranian attack as unprovoked and stated it "does not seek escalation" [13][26]. Iran's military accused the United States of breaching the ceasefire first by targeting an oil tanker and civilian areas including Qeshm Island, framing its own strikes as a justified response [4][6]. Iranian domestic media described the operation as a "پاسخ مقتدرانه" (authoritative response) to American violations [22]. CBS News reported that Trump called Iran's response to a US peace proposal "totally unacceptable" [21].

Security analysts characterized the exchange as calculated rather than a prelude to uncontrolled escalation. Donald Jensen, a former US diplomat, described the clash as a "controlled escalation" in which both nations are trying to demonstrate resolve while settling on a framework, predicting a limited resolution focused on strait passage rather than a comprehensive agreement [2]. Trita Parsi of the Quincy Institute said Iran likely perceives the latest US attacks as an effort to create "what Israel has created in Gaza, in the West Bank and in Lebanon, in which a ceasefire is essentially unilateral," though he added that both sides probably have an interest in preventing the situation from spiraling [2]. Chris Featherstone of the University of York said the violence demonstrates the conflict's volatility, with each side possibly seeking to use strikes to increase engagement with peace negotiations [2].

The broader diplomatic picture remains fractured. Iranian lawmaker Ebrahim Rezaei described the US 14-point ceasefire proposal — which demands a 12-year halt to enrichment and handover of Iran's 60-percent enriched uranium stockpile — as "more of an American wish-list than a reality" [1]. Iran's Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf mocked reports of a deal being close by writing "Operation Trust Me Bro failed" on social media [1]. Ali Vaez of the International Crisis Group told Al Jazeera that both sides would have to make painful concessions or leave main areas of disagreement vague to finalize any framework [1][2].

At the United Nations, Iran's permanent representative Amir Saeid Iravani called a US- and Bahrain-sponsored draft Security Council resolution on the Strait of Hormuz "deeply flawed, one-sided, and politically motivated," arguing it seeks to legitimize the US naval blockade [14]. Russia's UN mission urged Security Council members to refrain from "artificially stirring up tensions" and warned that confrontational resolutions could trigger further escalation, framing the entire conflict as "a direct consequence of unprovoked aggression by the US and Israel against Iran" [10]. Israel's Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu stated in a television interview that "the war in Iran is not over" until Iran's nuclear infrastructure and enriched uranium are eliminated, regardless of any maritime truce [27].

The economic fallout from the strait's near-total closure since late February continued to widen. Oil prices surged as much as 7.5 percent following the exchange of fire, with Brent crude reaching $103.70 before easing to $101.12 per barrel — roughly 40 percent above pre-war levels amid an estimated daily production shortfall of 14.5 million barrels [6]. At the ASEAN summit in Cebu, Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr called the situation a "limbo" and said peace is the fundamental first step, while the bloc called for "immediate cessation of hostilities" and restoration of safe transit passage [7][8]. Toyota forecast a 20-percent decline in annual operating profit, citing cost and supply uncertainties from the war [18]. Latin American economies face a 50-percent increase in international oil prices since February, with the regional energy organization OLACDE estimating that every $10 per barrel adds 0.2 percentage points to regional inflation [28].

The UAE reported intercepting two ballistic missiles and three drones launched from Iran on the same day, bringing its cumulative total since the conflict began to 551 ballistic missiles, 29 cruise missiles, and 2,263 drones [15]. French President Emmanuel Macron told his Iranian counterpart that strikes on Emirati civilian infrastructure were "unjustified" [4]. China's foreign ministry reported that an oil products tanker carrying Chinese crew was attacked in the strait, expressing deep concern [20]. The UN International Maritime Organization warned that approximately 1,500 ships and their crews remain stranded in the Gulf [4].

Analyst Negar Mortazavi said Tehran may show greater flexibility on its nuclear programme once the conflict ends but is unlikely to hand over enriched uranium directly to the United States [1][4]. The next expected development is Iran's formal response to the US ceasefire proposal, which Secretary of State Marco Rubio has said Washington is awaiting [31].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
