Turkish Riot Police Storm CHP Headquarters With Tear Gas and Rubber Bullets, Triggering Mass Protests

The police raid to enforce a court order reinstating former leader Kılıçdaroğlu marks a physical escalation of the opposition crisis, drawing thousands into the streets and sharp international criticism.

This article draws on 20 sources across six languages and nine countries, offering broad international coverage. However, no direct quotes appear from Kemal Kilicdaroğlu — the reinstated leader at the center of the dispute — nor from AKP ruling-party officials, Turkish judges, or financial analysts, leaving several key perspectives represented only through paraphrase or not at all. The article's own voice occasionally adopts charged language — such as 'political turmoil,' 'crisis,' and 'cracked down on' — that leans toward dramatizing events beyond what the sourced facts alone convey.

Hundreds of Turkish riot police forced their way into the headquarters of the Republican People's Party (CHP) in Ankara on May 24, using tear gas and rubber bullets to evict the party's ousted leadership after a court ruling annulled the 2023 party congress and reinstated former leader Kemal Kilicdaroğlu [1][2][3]. Supporters of ousted leader Özgür Özel barricaded the courtyard with buses and used fire extinguishers against officers, while police broke down doors and shattered ground-floor windows [4]. The Ankara Governor's Office stated it had authorized the operation to enforce the judicial decision [6][9].

The raid followed a court order that retroactively nullified the 2023 CHP leadership election on the basis of vote-buying allegations initially dismissed in October before being reopened [6]. Kilicdaroğlu's legal team had requested police assistance after stating they were barred from entering the building [1][4]. A separate Turkish legal analysis argued that disputes over party congress elections fall under the jurisdiction of the Supreme Election Council (YSK) rather than civil courts, questioning the legal foundation of the ruling [22].

Özgür Özel, who was barricaded on the 12th floor during the operation [5], posted a video declaring the party was "under attack" [1]. After leaving the building, he tore up the court order on camera and led an 8-kilometer march through heavy rain and hail to the gates of Parliament, where thousands of supporters had gathered [4][7]. He declared: "Je me révolte contre l'alliance de ceux qui veulent s'emparer du parti d'Atatürk et de ceux qui veulent livrer au pouvoir le parti d'Atatürk !" (I revolt against the alliance of those who want to seize Atatürk's party and those who want to deliver Atatürk's party to the government!) [5]. At Parliament he asked the crowd whether they were willing to rebuild the party for a third time [4].

The Turkish government framed the court decision as an independent judicial process. Justice Minister Akin Gürlek stated the appeal court ruling "reinforces our citizens' trust in democracy" [1]. President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, through the state-run Anadolu Agency, described the matter as judicial and said the government was not a party to it, urging that the Supreme Court appeal stage be awaited [10]. The government insisted that Turkey's courts act impartially and independently of political pressure [4].

Opposition figures and international observers drew a different conclusion. Human Rights Watch called the court order "the latest deeply damaging blow to the rule of law, democracy and human rights" in Turkey and warned of "abusive tactics" by the Erdoğan government to neutralize the CHP [3][5]. Al-Sharq Al-Awsat quoted Özel describing the ruling as a "judicial coup" and accusing Erdoğan of trying to turn the opposition into a "tame opposition" [13]. The DEM Party, Turkey's main pro-Kurdish political party, condemned the police intervention as an attack on democratic politics and called for opposition solidarity [11]. Al-Monitor framed the court ruling as part of a broader legal crackdown on the opposition [18].

Rank-and-file CHP members at the post-raid protests expressed confidence that Kilicdaroğlu lacked genuine support within the party. A supporter named Osman told RFI: "Eh, Kiliçdaroglu ! Organise ce congrès et vois ce qui se passe. Tu auras zéro voix" (Organize this congress and see what happens. You will get zero votes) [7]. Sevgi, a thirty-year party member, stated: "Le président essaie de gagner par des voies judiciaires les élections qu'il va perdre dans les urnes" (The president is trying to win through judicial means the elections he will lose at the ballot box) [7]. Özel was designated head of the CHP parliamentary group on Saturday, creating what RFI described as a party "with two heads" [5].

The crisis extended beyond the CHP. RFI's Ankara correspondent reported that police simultaneously cracked down on nearly a thousand students protesting a presidential decree closing Istanbul's private Bilgi University [5]. Student Asli Özkan described comrades being "jetés par dessus les tourniquets par la police qui leur serrait le cou" (thrown over the turnstiles by police who grabbed them by the neck), with one student suffering a broken nose [5]. Student unionist Emir Aydogan declared: "Nous resterons ici jusqu'à ce que l'université rouvre ses portes" (We will stay here until the university reopens) [5].

The political turmoil sent immediate shockwaves through Turkey's economy. Deutsche Welle's Turkish-language service reported that the lira hit record lows and the Istanbul stock exchange lost up to 15 percent of its value, with experts warning of lasting damage to investor confidence [15].

European institutions responded with criticism but faced constraints. The EU sharply criticized the police action, and German Foreign Minister Johann Wadephul expressed concern regarding Turkey's EU membership aspirations [6]. Politico Europe reported that Europe's dependence on Turkey for migration management, NATO security, and economic ties creates a multi-billion-euro dilemma that limits a stronger response [20].

The CHP has appealed the ruling to the Supreme Court, though legal experts told DW News that a decision could take over a year [2]. Both government and opposition circles expect early elections as soon as next year, rather than the scheduled 2028 date [7]. NBC News reported that observers view the corruption-focused cases against the CHP as aimed at weakening the opposition ahead of any vote, while the government maintains the judiciary is independent [17].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”
