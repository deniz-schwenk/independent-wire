Russia Demands Foreign Embassies Evacuate Kyiv Ahead of Threatened Retaliatory Strikes; EU and Germany Refuse

Moscow warns of inevitable attacks on Ukrainian 'decision-making centers' if Victory Day celebrations are disrupted, while Western governments call the demand an escalatory intimidation tactic.

May 7, 2026

This article draws on 26 sources across six languages and nine countries, though 18 of the 26 originate from Ukraine, which tilts the sourcing toward one party's perspective. The U.S. government's position on the Russian evacuation threat is entirely absent despite active American diplomatic engagement with Kyiv, and no non-Western embassies that received Moscow's note have responded publicly — leaving a significant gap in how the threat is perceived beyond the Western alliance. The article's own voice occasionally characterizes motives and significance (e.g., describing Russia's actions as projecting vulnerability, or calling Ukraine's reach 'expanding') rather than attributing those judgments to named analysts.

Russia's Foreign Ministry issued a formal note to foreign diplomatic missions urging them to evacuate personnel from Kyiv, warning of "inevitable" retaliatory strikes on the Ukrainian capital if the May 9 Victory Day parade is disrupted [1][17][18]. Spokeswoman Maria Zakharova stated the warning should be taken "very, very seriously," citing alleged Ukrainian "terrorist designs" against the celebrations [1]. The European Union and Germany publicly refused to comply, with German Foreign Minister Johann Wadephul declaring, "No, of course not. We are in close contact with other embassies. We won't be intimidated by this" [3].

The evacuation demand marks a new diplomatic dimension in the conflict, moving beyond the competing ceasefire announcements that both sides issued for the May 8–9 period. President Vladimir Putin announced a ceasefire covering Victory Day [23], while Ukraine rejected the pause, citing over 1,800 documented Russian violations of previous silence regimes [7][11]. The head of the UN Human Rights Monitoring Mission in Ukraine, Danielle Bell, expressed alarm over at least 70 civilians killed and more than 500 wounded since early May [11].

The Russian framing presents the threat as a protective measure rather than an escalatory act. Zakharova's statements, reported across French, German, Turkish, Latvian, and Chinese outlets, consistently characterize the warning as a consequence of Ukrainian provocations [17][25][26][23]. Chinese coverage on Netease emphasized the "retaliation" framing and noted the specific dates of competing ceasefire declarations [23]. Turkish outlet Yeni Şafak highlighted the formal diplomatic note and the threat to "decision-making centers" [25]. A German-language report framed the warning as a precursor to a potential "Großangriff" (major attack) on the Ukrainian capital [26].

Western institutions rejected this framing categorically. European Commission Spokesperson Anouar El Anouni stated that "Russia's public threats to attack Kyiv are part of its reckless, escalatory tactics" designed to shift blame onto Ukraine [7]. Wadephul characterized the demand as part of a hybrid war and confirmed Germany would maintain its diplomatic presence [3]. Neither the EU nor Germany indicated any change in their posture in Kyiv.

Ukrainian leadership dismissed the ceasefire proposal as cynical. President Volodymyr Zelensky stated: "They want Ukraine's permission to hold their parade – so they can safely take to the square for an hour once a year, and then go back to killing our people and waging war" [2]. Ukrainian Foreign Minister Andriy Sybiha accused Putin of caring only about "military parades, not human lives" [1]. Advisor to the Defense Minister Serhiy Sternenko confirmed that Ukrainian forces would not observe the May 9 ceasefire [21].

Meanwhile, Ukraine demonstrated expanding offensive reach. The commander of Ukraine's Unmanned Systems Forces, Robert Brovdi, confirmed a drone strike on the Lukoil-Permnefteorgsintez oil refinery in Perm on the night of May 6–7, describing how "Freedom-Loving Ukrainian Birds" targeted oil pumping and refining equipment over 1,500 kilometers from the front line [9][6]. Perm Governor Dmitry Makhonin acknowledged that one drone reached its target, though he reported no casualties [10]. Zelensky framed these operations as "long-range sanctions" targeting infrastructure financing Russian aggression, stating: "People must be valued, not parades. There is a need to establish peace, rather than running around the world's capitals begging for a pause on May 9" [8]. Ukrainian forces also struck a Project 22800 Karakurt missile ship capable of launching Kalibr missiles in Kaspiysk, Dagestan [6][20]. Independent military analyst Mick Ryan assessed that Ukrainian drones reaching 2,000 kilometers have eliminated safe rear areas for Russian aviation [22].

Russia's own domestic security measures underscored the vulnerability its threats sought to project outward. Moscow shut down mobile internet for the holiday, the annual military parade excluded vehicles for the first time in decades due to drone threats, and several Russian regions cancelled parades entirely [1][7].

On the ground in Ukraine, Russian strikes continued across multiple regions. A guided aerial bomb struck the village of Shchaslyve in Zaporizhzhia, injuring three elderly civilians [4]. In Kharkiv, a UAV strike on the Novobavarskyi district injured four people including a child and damaged at least 10 buildings [13]. The death toll from a May 4 Iskander missile strike on Merefa rose to eight after a 59-year-old man died of his injuries [14][24]. Russian forces carried out 41 FPV drone strikes on Chernihiv region border areas in a single 24-hour period [12]. Ukraine's power grid operator Ukrenergo reported new outages across five frontline regions from continued attacks on energy infrastructure [15]. Ukrainian air defenses intercepted 92 of 102 Russian drones launched overnight [16].

Separately, diplomatic engagement between Kyiv and Washington showed signs of revival. Rustem Umerov, Secretary of Ukraine's National Security and Defense Council, arrived in the United States ahead of scheduled talks with U.S. Special Envoy Steve Witkoff in Miami — the first such high-level contact in weeks [5][19]. Presidential communications adviser Dmytro Lytvyn confirmed Umerov had briefed Zelensky on the initial status of the mission [5]. The talks are expected to address security guarantees and prisoner exchanges [19].

No statements from the U.S. government on the Russian evacuation threat have been reported. Responses from non-Western embassies in Kyiv — including those of India, Japan, and Gulf states — remain publicly unknown despite being direct recipients of Moscow's diplomatic note.

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”

Stakeholder types

academia
Researchers, professors, think tanks, and university-based experts.
affected_community
People directly impacted by the events themselves — civilians, displaced persons, local populations. Voices from within the group, not their spokespersons.
civil_society
Non-state organizations representing collective interests (NGOs, human rights groups, trade unions, religious bodies).
government
Executive branch officials, ministries, heads of state, and their spokespersons.
industry
Private companies, trade associations, and commercial actors.
international_org
Multilateral bodies and their representatives (UN agencies, IMF, IAEA, Red Cross, regional alliances).
judiciary
Judges, courts, prosecutors, and legal bodies acting in their official capacity.
legislature
Parliament, Congress, or equivalent body. Kept separate from “government” because legislatures often hold positions that differ from their own executive branch.
media
Journalists, editorial boards, and outlets quoted for their position or analysis, not as sources of factual reporting.
military
Armed forces personnel, commanders, and defense ministries.
