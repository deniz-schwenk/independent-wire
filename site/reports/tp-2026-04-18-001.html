Iran Re-Closes Strait of Hormuz and Fires on Commercial Tanker, Reversing Brief Ceasefire Opening

The IRGC reimposed a full blockade on April 18 after accusing the United States of violating the terms of the strait's reopening, sending oil markets into renewed turmoil days after a 40-nation Paris summit sought a multinational plan to restore navigation.

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps reimposed a full closure of the Strait of Hormuz on April 18, 2026, reversing a brief reopening tied to the Lebanon-Israel ceasefire and reportedly firing on at least one commercial tanker attempting transit [1][7][11]. The re-closure came just one day after a 40-nation summit convened in Paris to coordinate a multinational mission to guarantee freedom of navigation through the waterway, which carries roughly 20 percent of the world's seaborne crude oil [8][9].

Iran had announced the conditional opening of the strait to merchant vessels on April 17, with Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi linking the move to the Lebanon-Israel ceasefire [2][3]. Hours later, Iran's joint military command reversed the decision, stating that the United States had violated the terms by refusing to lift its own blockade of Iranian ports [11]. The IRGC declared that the strait would remain under "strict management" and that no vessel would transit without Iranian authorization [1][11].

Persian-language outlet Didban Iran identified the tanker AUROURA by name as one of the vessels forced to turn back near the Musandam coast during the re-closure [7]. English-language sources referred more generally to "disruptions" and "reimposed restrictions" without naming specific affected ships [4][11]. The precise nature of the reported firing — whether naval gunfire, drone strike, or warning shots — remains unclear across available sources; most describe the IRGC "targeting" vessels rather than specifying the weapon system used [1][4][7].

The April 18 escalation follows an established pattern. In early March, the IRGC claimed to have struck more than 10 oil tankers that ignored transit bans in the strait, as reported by the Turkish defense outlet SDE: 'İran Hürmüz Boğazı'nda 10 Petrol Tankerini Vurduğunu Açıkladı' (Iran announced it struck over 10 oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz) [5]. That precedent — largely absent from Western and Chinese coverage of the April events — established the IRGC's willingness to use force against commercial shipping [5].

Iranian and Persian-language sources frame the closure as a defensive response to a U.S. maritime blockade of Iranian ports. Euronews Persian reported the IRGC's position that the strait would remain blocked as long as Iranian ports were under siege: 'سپاه بار دیگر تنگه هرمز را مسدود کرد' (The IRGC once again blocked the Strait of Hormuz) [1]. Tabnak framed the stalled Islamabad negotiations as a story of Iranian sovereignty, headlining its coverage 'نه ایران به زیاده خواهی آمریکا' (Iran's no to American greed) [10]. Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf asserted that jurisdiction over the strait belongs to Iran and warned that Tehran would not tolerate continued U.S. blockades, as reported by iFeng [6].

Western English-language sources frame the same events as aggressive disruptions of international navigation. Fortune reported that U.S. Admiral Brad Cooper confirmed American destroyers had transited the strait to begin mine clearance operations, presenting the U.S. military presence as a freedom-of-navigation mission [4]. KSAT, citing wire reports, described Iran as "reimposing restrictions" and "accusing the U.S. of violating the deal" — language that positions Iran as the party disrupting an agreed arrangement [11]. France and the United Kingdom demanded permanent freedom of navigation in the strait, according to VOA Chinese, though neither government's direct statements appeared in available sources [9].

Chinese-language sources foregrounded the economic consequences for energy markets. The 21st Century Business Herald reported that even during the nominally open ceasefire period, ships required explicit IRGC permission to transit — a detail that Western sources largely omitted when describing the opening as a general ceasefire concession [3]. The same outlet noted a 10 percent drop in international oil prices following the initial reopening announcement [3]. That relief proved short-lived: the U.S. Energy Information Administration had already raised its 2026 Brent crude forecast to $96 per barrel, citing the Hormuz chokepoint as a major supply disruption with outages peaking in April [8]. Goldman Sachs warned that another month of closure could push Brent above $100 per barrel for the remainder of 2026 [12].

The re-closure directly undercuts the Paris summit's objectives. The 40-nation meeting had aimed to assemble a "strictly peaceful and defensive" multinational mission to protect commercial shipping and support mine clearance, with the UK planning to deploy RFA Lyme Bay and mine-clearing assets from Bahrain [9]. Analysts had already cautioned that the coalition risked becoming a theoretical concept if security conditions did not stabilize [9].
