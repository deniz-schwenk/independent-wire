Berlin Donor Conference Pledges €1.5 Billion for Sudan as War Enters Fourth Year

Sudanese government rejects the conference as 'colonialist' while aid agencies warn pledges fall short of the world's largest humanitarian crisis.

Donors pledged approximately €1.5 billion at the International Sudan Conference in Berlin on April 15, 2026, marking three years since war erupted between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) [1][4]. Germany, which hosted the conference, contributed between €212 million and €230 million of the total, depending on the source — German-language outlets reported the lower figure while English-language reporting cited the higher [1][4][8]. The pledges exceeded an initial target of $1 billion but remain far below what aid organizations say is needed to address a crisis affecting more than 30 million people [9][11].

The United Nations reports that 14 million Sudanese — roughly a quarter of the population — have been displaced, including approximately 9 million internally displaced and 4.4 million as refugees in neighboring countries [12]. An estimated 21 million face acute food insecurity, including 6.3 million at emergency levels, and more than 200 attacks on health facilities have been documented since the war began in April 2023 [12]. Arabic-language Sudanese media describes conditions in starker terms: Al Taghier reported that civilians have resorted to eating tree leaves and that 61.7% of the population faces food insecurity — 'السودان على حافة المجاعة' (Sudan is on the brink of famine) [7]. Analyst Eric Reeves documented famine spreading to specific locations in North Darfur, including Um Baru and Kernoi, alongside the collapse of the health system in those areas [6].

The conference's framing diverges sharply depending on the source. German Foreign Minister Wadephul described the Sudan conflict as a 'brutal catastrophe' and called for a global weapons embargo to halt the flow of modern arms to both warring parties [4]. UN High Commissioner for Human Rights Volker Türk echoed this demand, criticizing the use of drones and modern weapons in the conflict [4]. German Development Minister Alabali Radovan warned against the world forgetting the Sudan crisis, emphasizing the need for basic services including water and education, and highlighting systematic sexual violence as a defining feature of the war — a dimension largely absent from non-German coverage [8].

The Sudanese government rejected the conference outright. As El Demócrata reported: 'Sudán califica de inaceptable y colonialista la conferencia humanitaria de Berlín' (Sudan labels the Berlin humanitarian conference unacceptable and colonialist) [3]. Khartoum accused the organizers of violating national sovereignty and legitimizing militias [3]. Separately, the Sudanese Prime Minister characterized the conference results and pledges as non-binding, as reported by Radio Dabanga: 'مؤتمر برلين يخرج بتعهدات تبلغ 1.5 مليار دولار لدعم السودان' (The Berlin conference produces pledges of $1.5 billion to support Sudan) [5]. The Spanish-language source amplified the sovereignty framing prominently in its headline, while European sources subordinated it to the humanitarian narrative [3][4][8].

The African Union offered qualified support. Le Monde reported that Mahamoud Ali Youssouf, President of the African Union Commission, welcomed the German initiative but stressed the critical absence of a cessation of hostilities: 'Après trois ans de guerre au Soudan, la conférence de Berlin recueille plus de 1,3 milliard d'euros de promesses d'aide' (After three years of war in Sudan, the Berlin conference collects more than €1.3 billion in aid pledges) [2]. This tension — between welcoming funds and noting the lack of a ceasefire — reflects a broader structural problem with the conference.

Chinese-language coverage foregrounded a different concern entirely. Artto News emphasized that neither the SAF nor the RSF participated in the Berlin conference, treating this exclusion as the primary reason peace prospects remain dim and the pledges as secondary to the political failure [9]. RTE and DW confirmed that both warring parties were deliberately excluded, with the conference instead including approximately 40 political and civilian figures [10][11]. This framing contrasts with Western European sources, which acknowledged the absence but centered the humanitarian funding narrative [1][4].

Amnesty International introduced an accountability dimension absent from most government and mainstream media coverage. The organization noted that less than 40% of the 2025 humanitarian response plan for Sudan had been funded, and urged high-income countries to address NGO operational disruptions that prevent aid from reaching affected populations [13]. This record of past shortfalls raises questions about whether the new pledges will translate into actual disbursements — a concern that no conference-focused source addressed in detail.

This report draws on 13 sources in six languages (English, French, German, Arabic, Spanish, and Chinese). No direct testimony from Sudanese civilians, displaced persons, or famine-affected communities was available despite their being the central subjects of the conference and the crisis. No perspectives from either the SAF or RSF — the two belligerents whose actions created the crisis and whose absence from Berlin is itself contested — were represented. No voices from frontline humanitarian organizations such as Médecins Sans Frontières or the Sudanese Red Crescent, who could speak to the gap between pledges and actual aid delivery, were found. Perspectives from regional governments with direct stakes — Egypt, Chad, South Sudan, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE, several of which host large refugee populations or face accusations of arms supply — were also absent.

The conference concluded with no ceasefire agreement and no mechanism to compel the warring parties to negotiate [2][9]. Whether the €1.5 billion in pledges will be disbursed, and whether aid can reach besieged areas where famine conditions are confirmed, remains unresolved [6][13]. The next expected development is the implementation of pledged funds against a backdrop of continued fighting and restricted humanitarian access [12][13].

About these labels

Not every tag needs a definition — those listed below cover the full vocabulary used across the dossier.

Divergence types

factual
Sources disagree on a verifiable fact: a date, number, name, or whether something happened.
framing
Sources describe the same event using different language or implied meaning. Example: one outlet calls a payment “compensation,” another calls it “sanctions relief.”
omission
One or more sources report something that other sources leave out entirely.
emphasis
Sources cover the same event but give different aspects different weight or prominence. Example: one outlet leads with casualty figures; another treats them as a footnote to the political negotiations.

Bias issues

evaluative_adjective
A descriptive word that signals the writer’s judgment rather than a neutral fact. Examples: “staggering,” “sharp,” “dramatic.”
intensifier
A word that amplifies a statement without adding information. Examples: “very,” “extremely,” “deeply.”
loaded_term
Vocabulary carrying strong political or emotional connotations that a more neutral word would avoid. Examples: “regime” vs. “government,” “crackdown” vs. “enforcement.”
hedging
Phrases that soften or obscure a claim, making attribution less clear. Examples: “some say,” “allegedly,” “reportedly.”
