Nearly 50 European leaders gathered in Yerevan on May 4, 2026, for the eighth summit of the European Political Community, marking the first time the forum has convened in the South Caucasus [7][8]. Armenian media described the event as the largest diplomatic gathering the country has hosted since independence [7], while European Council President António Costa emphasized the geographic significance of holding the meeting in the Caucasus region [8].

The summit unfolded against a backdrop of accelerating transatlantic strain. President Donald Trump ordered the withdrawal of more than 5,000 troops from Germany following tensions with Chancellor Friedrich Merz over Iran policy [10]. German Defence Minister Boris Pistorius responded that the drawdown was foreseeable and that NATO must become "more European" to remain transatlantic, calling on member states to assume greater responsibility for their own defense [9]. EU-level reporting echoed this framing, emphasizing energy independence and the diversification of global partnerships as core summit objectives [6].

Trump's friction extended beyond Germany. He publicly stated that "Italy has not been of any help to us" and threatened to pull American forces from the country [4]. US Secretary of State Marco Rubio was dispatched to Rome and the Vatican to manage the fallout, which also included disagreements with Pope Leo XIV over US-Israeli military strikes on Iran and immigration policy [4]. The pattern of American unilateralism provided an immediate context for the Yerevan summit's defense-autonomy agenda.

For Armenia, hosting the summit carried a distinct geopolitical message. Russian-language reporting framed the event as a deliberate Western strategy to draw Yerevan out of Moscow's orbit [13]. ArmInfo reported that one of Armenia's specific goals tied to the summit was phasing out Russian border guard services along its frontiers [11]. A former Armenian foreign minister, however, warned that the gathering could provoke diplomatic friction, particularly given Azerbaijan's refusal to attend and the broader sensitivities of the South Caucasus security environment [12].

Turkish officials used the occasion to advance a normalization narrative. Vice President Cevdet Yilmaz met Armenian Prime Minister Nikol Pashinyan on the summit's sidelines, and the two sides signed a memorandum to restore the ancient Ani Bridge spanning the border [3]. Yilmaz stated that the normalization process between Türkiye and Armenia is "extremely valuable" and "will serve as an example and inspiration for others" [3]. He also credited Azerbaijani President Ilham Aliyev for contributing to regional peace, though Baku itself declined to participate in the summit [3][12].

President Recep Tayyip Erdogan, speaking separately, framed Türkiye's role in broader terms. "Let it not be forgotten that Türkiye is no longer the old Türkiye, nor is the world still confined to the sphere of influence of Western states," he said, urging the EU to abandon what he called "strategic myopia" regarding Ankara's membership bid [2]. Erdogan positioned European cooperation with Türkiye as a necessity for Europe's continued relevance as a global actor rather than a concession to Ankara [2].

The summit's regional context extended to the wider Middle East. Turkish Foreign Minister Hakan Fidan and his Qatari counterpart Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al Thani discussed US-Iran negotiations and a new wave of Iranian missile and drone attacks targeting the UAE, with both ministers expressing concern about regional stability and the risk of further escalation [1]. Separately, Rome prosecutors opened an investigation into the Israeli military's interception of the Global Sumud Flotilla, an Italian-flagged vessel carrying humanitarian aid to Gaza that was boarded in international waters near Crete, with prosecutors opening a probe against Israeli forces on suspicion of kidnapping, robbery, and causing damage with risk of shipwreck under Italian law, while the two Italian activists aboard are the alleged victims in the case [5].

French President Emmanuel Macron arrived in Yerevan for the discussions [8], underscoring Paris's continued engagement with Armenia — a relationship that has deepened as Yerevan's ties with Moscow have frayed. The summit's agenda included sessions on energy security and collective defense planning, though detailed outcomes had not been publicly released at the time of reporting [6].

The Yerevan gathering exposed a central tension: whether Armenia's European turn can be sustained without provoking further instability in a region where Russian, Turkish, and Azerbaijani interests intersect. Russian media characterized the summit as a geopolitical extraction operation [13], while Armenian critics cautioned that the diplomatic benefits must be weighed against the risks of alienating neighbors [12]. European participants, for their part, focused on the summit as a vehicle for defense self-reliance in an era of diminishing American guarantees [9][6]. No statements from NATO, the OSCE, or the CSTO regarding the summit's impact on existing collective security arrangements were available in the reporting reviewed.
